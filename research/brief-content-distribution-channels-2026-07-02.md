## Competitor Content Gaps

Five gaps no competitor currently covers:

### Gap 1: No channel selection framework
Every competitor lists the same channels without helping the reader decide which to prioritize. Channel fit depends on: audience type (enterprise vs. SMB, technical vs. marketing buyer), content format (long-form vs. data-driven vs. visual), team bandwidth, and existing owned audience size. No competitor provides a decision framework — only exhaustive lists that imply you should activate all of them.

### Gap 2: No B2B-specific benchmarks per channel
A LinkedIn post that gets 3,000 impressions — is that good or bad for B2B SaaS? An email blast with 28% open rate — strong or weak? A Reddit post in a SaaS subreddit with 47 upvotes — is that worth the effort? No competitor answers these calibration questions. Without benchmarks, B2B content teams have no way to evaluate channel performance or decide what to keep vs. cut.

### Gap 3: No per-channel activation playbook
Competitors describe channels conceptually ("use LinkedIn to share your content with your network"). No competitor goes into the specific moves: for LinkedIn, native long-form post vs. carousel vs. short hook + link, optimal comment seeding window (within 60 minutes of posting), DM outreach to first-degree connections who engaged, employee advocacy activation sequence. The gap between "use this channel" and "here's exactly how to activate it" is where most distribution efforts fail.

### Gap 4: No content-channel fit by format
Research reports, how-to guides, case studies, data studies, opinion posts, and tool comparisons each have different natural distribution channels. Research reports earn press coverage and academic shares; data studies attract backlinks; case studies convert in email sequences; how-to guides generate search traffic and Pinterest pins; opinion posts drive LinkedIn engagement. No competitor maps content format to highest-leverage channel, so marketers default to distributing everything everywhere (inefficient) or distributing on one channel regardless of fit (low ROI).

### Gap 5: No "minimum viable distribution stack" for small teams
Most distribution guides assume you have a team to execute across 8–12 channels. For a B2B SaaS with a 1–2 person content team, that's noise. No competitor answers the real question for most B2B content teams: "If I can only commit to 3 channels consistently, which 3 should they be and how much time does each take?"

## Differentiating Angle

Most content distribution guides are channel encyclopedias — exhaustive lists with shallow descriptions. They tell you every possible channel exists; they don't help you choose.

This article is a channel selection guide for B2B SaaS teams with finite resources. The core argument: most B2B content teams should activate 3–4 channels and do them well, not spread thinly across 12.

The article provides:
1. A channel selection matrix — score channels by audience fit, content format fit, and available resources to find your priority stack
2. Per-channel activation playbooks for the 6 channels that matter most for B2B SaaS (LinkedIn, email, LinkedIn groups/Slack communities, journalist seeding, content syndication, Reddit)
3. B2B benchmarks for each channel — what "good" looks like so teams can calibrate
4. Minimum viable distribution stack — the 3-channel stack for teams with under 5 hours/week for distribution
