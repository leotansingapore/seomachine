## Competitor Gap Analysis (Top 10 SERP)

### Gap 1: Onboarding defined only as the first-login flow
Every top-10 competitor treats onboarding as the first-session experience — welcome modal, product tour, checklist. None map the full onboarding arc: sign-up → activation → habit formation → power user. The real drop-off is at activation (getting users to first value), not at the welcome email screen. Teams optimizing the welcome modal are fixing the wrong stage.

### Gap 2: No PLG vs. SLG architecture separation
PLG onboarding (self-serve, product-first, event-driven, activation-metric-led) and SLG onboarding (CSM-led, milestone-driven, high-touch, relationship-first) are fundamentally different architectures. Most guides conflate them or describe a generic hybrid without the decision logic. The right onboarding architecture follows your GTM motion — no existing top-10 guide explains this.

### Gap 3: No activation framework
Competitors list tactics: add tooltips, write a welcome email, build a checklist. None provide the structural framework: identify aha moment → define signal events → build minimum activation sequence → measure activation rate → iterate. Without the framework, teams can't prioritize which tactics to implement or in what order.

### Gap 4: No activation rate measurement guidance
"Activation rate" appears in most guides but is never defined with enough precision to be actionable. No guide explains the signal event approach (a specific in-product action that predicts long-term retention), how to identify the signal event from cohort data, how to calculate activation rate, or what a healthy benchmark looks like by product type. Teams end up tracking "completed onboarding checklist" instead of "took the action that predicts 90-day retention."

### Gap 5: No onboarding audit methodology
All guides assume you're designing onboarding from scratch. No guide provides a diagnostic framework for teams with existing onboarding: how to identify where users drop off, which metric to check first, how to categorize failure modes (friction, empty state problem, mis-defined aha moment, wrong trigger timing), and how to prioritize fixes by leverage.

## Differentiating Angle

SaaS onboarding is not a welcome sequence. It is a value architecture problem.

Most products deliver features during onboarding — they show users what the product can do rather than guiding them to the specific outcome they signed up for, as fast as possible. The result is users who understand the product but never use it.

The framework this article introduces:
1. Identify your aha moment (the specific action that predicts 90-day retention)
2. Design the minimum activation sequence (remove every step that isn't required to reach aha moment)
3. Build the value ladder (empty state → first value → repeated value → expansion)
4. Measure activation rate using signal events, not checklist completion

This is a structural approach competitors don't offer. It gives readers a diagnostic process, not just a tactics list.
