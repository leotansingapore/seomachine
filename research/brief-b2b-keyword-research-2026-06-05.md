## Competitor Gap Analysis

**Competitors ranking for this keyword**: Semrush, Ahrefs, HubSpot, Neil Patel, Backlinko, Demand Gen Report

### What every competitor does
- Lists keyword research tools (Semrush, Ahrefs, Google Keyword Planner) and walks through finding volume/KD
- Applies generic B2C keyword intent framework (informational / commercial / transactional) to B2B context without modification
- Focuses on one persona — the "decision-maker" — ignoring that B2B purchases involve multiple stakeholders
- Recommends targeting high-volume keywords without accounting for B2B's volume paradox (low volume, high value)

### Five gaps no competitor fills

**Gap 1: No buyer journey keyword mapping framework**  
Every guide says "map keywords to buyer intent." None show what that actually looks like for B2B's six-stage buyer journey: Problem Awareness → Solution Awareness → Category Definition → Provider Comparison → Validation → Procurement. Each stage generates structurally different query patterns. No competitor provides a practical mapping table a marketer can implement.

**Gap 2: No multi-stakeholder keyword architecture**  
In B2B, 5+ people influence a purchase decision. The Head of Marketing searches differently than the CTO, who searches differently than the CFO, who searches differently than the end user. No existing B2B keyword research guide covers multi-stakeholder keyword mapping: how to identify what each persona searches for, when in the journey, and how to build content that reaches all of them.

**Gap 3: No volume paradox explanation**  
B2B keywords have low search volume and high deal value. A keyword with 100 searches/month that maps to a $50K ACV deal is worth more than a keyword with 10,000 searches/month that maps to no purchase intent. No competitor explains how to think about volume in the context of pipeline value — the calculation that actually drives ROI decisions.

**Gap 4: No dark funnel keyword strategy**  
70% of B2B research happens before a buyer ever contacts a vendor. Dark funnel keywords (symptom searches, anonymous research queries) represent the majority of B2B keyword opportunity and the most defensible moat — no competitor can outbid you for organic rankings. No guide covers how to systematically find and target dark funnel keywords.

**Gap 5: No implementation priority framework**  
Even guides that cover good principles leave marketers with a flat list of keywords and no guidance on sequencing. Which keywords should be targeted first — by stage, by persona, by competitive difficulty, by pipeline proximity? No competitor provides a priority scoring model that accounts for B2B-specific variables.

## Differentiating Angle

**Frame B2B keyword research as pipeline mapping, not search volume chasing.**

The core argument: B2B keyword research isn't about finding keywords with the highest search volume. It's about mapping every stage of your buyer's journey to the specific queries they use — across every stakeholder involved in the purchase decision — and building content that intercepts buyers at each stage before they reach a competitor.

The deliverable: A practical, implementable keyword mapping framework a B2B marketer can use in a spreadsheet, not just principles to think about.

## Section Outline

### Section 1: Why B2B Keyword Research Is Different
- The volume paradox: low search volume ≠ low value in B2B
- The B2B buyer journey: six stages vs. B2C's one or two
- The multi-stakeholder dynamic: 5.4 people involved in average B2B tech purchase
- Dark funnel reality: 70% of research happens before first vendor contact
- The implication: how each of these differences changes your keyword strategy

### Section 2: The Six-Stage Buyer Journey Keyword Map
Practical mapping table — for each stage, provide:
- Stage name and definition
- Query pattern type and examples
- Search intent signal
- Content type that matches
- Typical search volume range (B2B: expect 10x lower than B2C equivalents)
- Priority in pipeline terms

**Stage 1 — Problem Awareness**
- Query pattern: symptom and pain queries
- Examples: "why is our churn rate increasing", "sales team hitting quota problems", "B2B lead generation not working"
- Intent: seeking diagnosis, not solution
- Content: educational blog posts, framework explainers
- Volume: typically 50-500/mo (very low, very high intent)

**Stage 2 — Solution Awareness**
- Query pattern: solution category queries
- Examples: "CRM software", "sales enablement platform", "revenue operations tools"
- Intent: understanding what category of solution exists
- Content: category overview, comparison of approach types
- Volume: 1K-50K/mo (competitive, high funnel)

**Stage 3 — Category Definition**
- Query pattern: "what is X", "how does X work", definitional queries
- Examples: "what is revenue intelligence", "how does ABM work", "what is PLG"
- Intent: building understanding of the solution space
- Content: educational pillar content, glossary entries
- Volume: 500-5K/mo (medium)

**Stage 4 — Provider Comparison**
- Query pattern: alternatives, comparisons, "best X"
- Examples: "[Product] vs [Product]", "best CRM for B2B", "[Product] alternatives"
- Intent: actively evaluating vendors
- Content: comparison pages, alternatives pages, feature comparison tables
- Volume: 100-2K/mo (medium, extremely high purchase intent)

**Stage 5 — Validation**
- Query pattern: review queries, social proof, proof of outcomes
- Examples: "[Product] reviews", "[Product] G2", "[Product] case studies", "[Product] customers"
- Intent: confirming the decision
- Content: case studies, testimonials, G2/Capterra optimization, ROI calculators
- Volume: 100-1K/mo (brand-specific, high close-rate)

**Stage 6 — Procurement**
- Query pattern: branded + feature + compliance queries
- Examples: "[Product] pricing", "[Product] security", "[Product] SOC 2", "[Product] API docs"
- Intent: operational/procurement due diligence
- Content: pricing pages, security documentation, API documentation, compliance pages
- Volume: varies (branded: 50-5K/mo)

### Section 3: Multi-Stakeholder Keyword Mapping
- Why you need keywords for each stakeholder, not just the "decision-maker"
- Worked example: a $50K SaaS deal involves — Head of Marketing (ROI + attribution), CTO (security + API), CFO (pricing + compliance), End User (features + UX), Procurement (vendor risk)
- How to build a stakeholder × journey stage matrix
- Practical: how to use LinkedIn + forums + sales call recordings to identify stakeholder-specific queries

### Section 4: Finding B2B Keywords in Practice
- Tool stack: Semrush or Ahrefs (primary), Google Search Console (for existing signals), Google Keyword Planner (free baseline), People Also Ask + autocomplete (dark funnel clues), Reddit/LinkedIn/industry forums (stakeholder language)
- Process: seed keywords → expand by stage → filter for B2B intent signals → cluster by stage and persona
- Dark funnel keyword discovery: how to find Problem Awareness queries that tools undercount (Reddit, Quora, G2/Capterra reviews, sales call analysis)
- Volume paradox scoring: how to weight low-volume B2B keywords by pipeline proximity, not raw search count

### Section 5: Priority Scoring for B2B Keywords
- The five variables that matter: Pipeline stage (BOFU weighted 3×), Buyer intent signal strength, Current ranking position (existing momentum), Competitive difficulty, Content gap (do you have anything near this keyword?)
- Simple priority score: (Pipeline Stage Weight × Intent Score) ÷ KD × Momentum Bonus
- How to sequence content: start BOFU (Stage 4-6), build MOFU (Stage 2-3), TOFU last (Stage 1)
- Counterintuitive: why most B2B companies should start with comparison pages, not blog posts

### Section 6: Building Your B2B Keyword Map (Practical Template)
- Five-column keyword map: Keyword | Journey Stage | Stakeholder Persona | Current Ranking | Priority Score
- How to populate it: start with 25 seed keywords, expand to 100+, score and filter to 30 actionable targets
- Cluster formation: group by stage and intent, not just by topic
- How to use the map to brief content production (hand off to writers with stage + persona context)
