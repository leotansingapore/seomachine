## 2. Competitive Landscape

### Top Competitor Approaches
1. **Generic CRO platforms (Unbounce, Instapage, Leadpages)**: Publish best-practice lists aimed at their own users — lead gen landing pages, paid ad landing pages, single-product e-commerce. Not B2B SaaS-specific. Assume one buyer, single session, impulse-adjacent decision.

2. **SaaS-adjacent product blogs (Intercom, Hotjar, Appcues)**: Focus on in-app or post-signup UX. Rarely cover top-of-funnel landing pages. When they do, it's for PLG free-trial pages — not the full B2B buyer decision context.

3. **SEO/content blogs (Backlinko, CXL, ConversionXL)**: More rigorous on evidence — heatmap data, A/B test results, specific copy formulas. But generic: no distinction between PLG and SLG landing pages, no buying committee coverage, no B2B SaaS-specific signals (SOC 2 badges, G2 reviews, named customer logos with ARR context).

### Common Sections (must cover)
- Hero section (headline, subheadline, CTA, hero image/visual)
- Value proposition clarity
- Social proof types (logos, testimonials, reviews, case study snippets)
- Form optimization (fields, friction, placement)
- CTA design and copy
- Page speed and mobile

### Content Gaps (differentiation opportunities)
1. **No multi-evaluator architecture**: Every competitor assumes a single buyer makes the decision in one session. B2B SaaS buying committees have 5-7 stakeholders who often review the landing page independently. A page that converts the champion (technical proof, integration detail) fails the CFO (ROI framing, pricing transparency) and IT (security documentation, compliance badges). No competitor maps section-level design to each evaluator.

2. **No PLG vs. SLG distinction**: A free-trial SaaS landing page has fundamentally different conversion mechanics than a demo-request page. PLG landing pages need to reduce activation friction (fewer form fields, clear onboarding promise). SLG pages need to reduce evaluation anxiety (reference customers, transparent process, discovery call expectations). No guide distinguishes the two.

3. **No intent-matched page architecture**: Most landing pages treat all visitors the same. But a visitor arriving from a branded search ("Leo Tan SEO Agency") is at a different buying stage than one arriving from "SEO agency for B2B SaaS." Page architecture -- especially CTA hierarchy -- should reflect this. No competitor maps landing page design to the intent of the incoming traffic source.

4. **No specificity standards for social proof**: Every guide says "add social proof." None specifies what makes proof credible to a B2B SaaS buyer: named customers (not just logos), ARR range or company size context, specific outcome metrics ("reduced time-to-rank by 40%"), review scores with volume (not cherry-picked). The standard is much higher for B2B than B2C.

5. **No trust signal integration for B2B buyers**: SOC 2 Type II badges, GDPR compliance, named CSM assignment, SLA commitments -- these are conversion elements for enterprise SaaS, not decorative trust signals. No landing page guide explains where to place them, which evaluators they're for, or how to test their impact.

## 3. Differentiating Angle

**The core idea**: Most SaaS landing pages are designed for one buyer making one decision. B2B SaaS pages are reviewed by 5-7 people across multiple sessions. The best-practice that competitors miss is multi-evaluator page architecture: structuring the page so each stakeholder finds their primary trust signal without overwhelming any single visitor.

**The framing**: "Your landing page isn't just a page — it's a sales document reviewed by a committee. Design it like one."

Three organizing principles:
1. **Above-the-fold belongs to the champion** (technical buyer, initiator of evaluation) — immediate value clarity, integration specifics, "try it" CTA
2. **Mid-page belongs to the economic buyer** (CFO, VP Revenue) — ROI framing, customer outcomes with metrics, pricing transparency signal, named reference logos
3. **Bottom-of-page belongs to gatekeepers** (IT, procurement, legal) — SOC 2 badge, GDPR compliance, SLA, security FAQ link, vendor due-diligence materials

Plus: the PLG vs. SLG fork — different above-the-fold design philosophy depending on whether you're optimizing for trial starts or demo requests.

- What this guide covers: section-by-section architecture for multi-stakeholder pages

> **Key Takeaways** block (4–5 bullets)

H2: PLG vs. SLG: Two Landing Page Philosophies
H3: PLG (free trial) landing pages
  - Goal: minimize friction to first activation event
  - Above-the-fold: immediate value, single-field form, social proof of time-to-value
  - Key spec: 1-field signup (email only), no credit card required badge, time-to-value promise
  - Common mistake: feature list instead of outcome claim in hero

H3: SLG (demo request) landing pages  
  - Goal: reduce evaluation anxiety, qualify the right leads
  - Above-the-fold: specific outcome claim, named customer logos, process transparency ("what happens after you book")
  - Key spec: 3-5 field form (name, company, role, size, challenge), Calendly embed vs. form debate
  - Common mistake: aggressive demo CTA on traffic that isn't ready to evaluate

H2: The Multi-Evaluator Page Architecture
H3: Above the fold — designed for the champion
  - Champion profile: technical evaluator, problem owner, evaluation initiator
  - What they need: specific outcome claim (not generic "grow your business"), integration context, see-it-in-action proof, low-friction CTA
  - Above-the-fold formula: [Specific outcome] for [ICP] who [existing situation]. [Sub: How it works in one line.] [CTA]. [Social proof anchor: X companies / Y reviews / G2 badge]
  - Do NOT: list features, use vague superlatives ("the best"), bury the CTA below the fold

H3: Mid-page — designed for the economic buyer
  - Economic buyer profile: CFO, VP Revenue, budget holder
  - What they need: business case framing, cost of inaction, outcome metrics from named customers, ROI signal, pricing visibility
  - Mid-page sections: customer outcome stats (specific metric, not "improved efficiency"), pricing transparency signal ("starts at X" or "see plans" link -- not hiding pricing entirely), named customer case study snippet with company size context
  - The ROI anchor: one-line revenue or cost impact stat that the economic buyer can cite internally

H3: Bottom-of-page — designed for gatekeepers
  - Gatekeeper profiles: IT security, procurement, legal, compliance
  - What they need: SOC 2 badge (Type II preferred), GDPR/CCPA compliance statement, SLA specification, security documentation link, vendor evaluation pack (RFP template, security questionnaire)
  - Placement: footer section before the final CTA -- don't bury in a page tab or separate URL
  - Common mistake: treating security badges as decorative; they are conversion elements for IT evaluators

H2: Section-by-Section Best Practices
H3: Hero section
  - Headline formula: [Specific outcome] for [ICP] — avoid generic verbs (transform, accelerate, revolutionize)
  - Subheadline: one sentence on mechanism (how you achieve the outcome), not a feature list
  - CTA: verb + specific outcome ("Get my free SEO audit", not "Get started"); single primary CTA above fold
  - Hero visual: show the product interface in context of the outcome, not a stock photo or abstract illustration

H3: Social proof
  - B2B specificity standards: named customers (not just logos), company size/ARR context, specific outcome metrics
  - Placement: anchor proof element immediately below CTA (not after a long feature section)
  - Review widgets: G2/Capterra/Trustpilot badges with score + volume (not just score)
  - What NOT to do: anonymous "small business owner" testimonials, round-number claims ("200% ROI"), logos without context

H3: Feature section
  - Lead with outcomes, list features as the mechanism
  - Format: outcome headline → feature list as evidence (not the reverse)
  - B2B SaaS error: 8-feature grid with icons but no connection to buyer problems

H3: Form design
  - PLG: 1-2 fields max (email only preferred), no credit card required badge, progress indicator if multi-step
  - SLG: 4-6 fields (qualify with company size and role), set expectations for what happens after submission
  - Error state: specific (not "invalid email"), friendly
  - Post-submit: redirect to calendar booking, not a "thank you" dead end

H3: CTA hierarchy
  - Primary CTA: one clear action (trial or demo, not both)
  - Secondary CTA: lower-commitment alternative (case study download, ROI calculator, pricing) -- only if primary CTA resistance is high
  - Repeated CTAs: 3 placements recommended (above fold, after social proof section, end of page)

H2: What to Test (and in what order)
  - Test 1: Headline specificity (generic vs. ICP-specific claim)
  - Test 2: Above-the-fold CTA copy (verb + outcome vs. generic)
  - Test 3: Social proof type (logos vs. testimonial quotes vs. metric claims)
  - Test 4: Form field count (PLG: email-only vs. email + name)
  - Test 5: Security/trust badge placement (mid-page vs. footer)
  - What NOT to test first: button color, font size, image choices -- these move CVR fractions; headline and proof type move it by percentage points

H2: SaaS Landing Page Audit Checklist (10-point)
  1. Does your headline name a specific outcome (not a generic verb)?
  2. Is your primary CTA above the fold?
  3. Do your customer logos include company size or named testimonials?
  4. Is your form field count appropriate for PLG vs. SLG?
  5. Do you have a post-submit flow (calendar, confirmation email, next steps)?
  6. Is your SOC 2 / security badge visible without scrolling to the footer?
  7. Do you have a mid-page ROI anchor (outcome stat + named customer)?
  8. Does your feature section lead with outcomes, not feature names?
  9. Is your CTA repeated at least 3 times on the page?
  10. Does your mobile layout preserve the above-fold CTA?

Conclusion (150–200 words)
  - The multi-evaluator frame: design for the committee, not the individual
  - Where to start: run the audit checklist, fix above-the-fold headline and CTA first
  - Connect to CRO pillar: Layer 2 (page conversion) is one of four; don't neglect intent audit
  - CTA: /contact — talk to our team about a SaaS landing page audit

FAQ Section (4–5 questions, PAA targets)
- What should be above the fold on a SaaS landing page?
- How many form fields should a SaaS landing page have?
- What social proof works best for B2B SaaS landing pages?
- How do I optimize a SaaS landing page for demo conversions?
- What's the difference between a PLG and SLG landing page?
