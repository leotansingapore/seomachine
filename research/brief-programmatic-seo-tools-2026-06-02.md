## Competitor Analysis

### Current SERP landscape
Two dominant content patterns:
1. **Enterprise-heavy roundups**: Lists of 15–25 tools across vague categories ("data tools", "CMS tools", "analytics tools"), most requiring an engineering team. No guidance on which tools to use for your scale.
2. **Tool vendor articles**: Written by SaaS companies (usually headless CMS vendors) that position their own product first and fill in the rest generically.

### Key gaps in all competitors
1. **No stack guidance by scale**: Same tool list whether you're a solo founder or an enterprise engineering team. The non-engineer path is never explained.
2. **No phase-based organization**: Tools aren't organized by which layer of the programmatic SEO system they support (data, template, publishing, monitoring). This makes articles impossible to act on.
3. **No honest tradeoffs**: Nobody says "Webflow CMS is fine for 200 pages but will degrade at 2,000" or "Notion as a data source works for prototyping but not production."
4. **No minimum viable stack**: What's the minimum you need to test programmatic SEO before committing engineering time? No competitor answers this.
5. **Missing data flow explanation**: Individual tool reviews, but no description of how data flows from a spreadsheet through a template into published pages.

---

## Differentiation Strategy

Organize by the four layers of a programmatic SEO system rather than by tool category:
1. **Data layer** — where structured data lives
2. **Template layer** — where page structure and content logic is defined
3. **Publishing layer** — what generates pages and makes them crawlable
4. **Monitoring layer** — what tracks indexation, rankings, and conversions

For each layer: one recommendation for non-engineers (no-code/low-code), one for developers. Honest tradeoffs at each option. Close with the two stacks assembled end-to-end.

Lead with: the tools question is secondary to the architecture question. Understanding the four layers first is what makes tool selection actionable.
