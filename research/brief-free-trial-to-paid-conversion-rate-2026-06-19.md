## Competitor content gaps

Five gaps not covered by any top-10 competitor:

1. **Treating conversion as a single event, not a sequence.** Every competitor gives tips for "the moment of conversion." But the path from trial signup to paid has 4–6 distinct micro-conversions (activation, habit formation, limit encounter, upgrade consideration, payment completion), each with different drop-off drivers and different optimization levers. No guide maps this full sequence.

2. **No distinction between trial expiry conversion vs. limit-hit conversion.** These are fundamentally different psychological moments. Trial expiry ("your 14 days are up") creates an artificial deadline; limit-hit conversion ("you've used your 3 free reports") creates a natural value signal. The optimization levers — urgency framing, upgrade prompt design, email timing — are completely different. No competitor separates them.

3. **No upgrade prompt design guidance.** The in-product upgrade prompt is the highest-conversion touchpoint for PLG SaaS, and no guide covers it. What should the modal say? When should it appear? What's the right call-to-action copy for a user who just hit a feature gate vs. a usage cap? This is a significant gap.

4. **No benchmark breakdown by trial model.** The standard "2–5% is good" benchmark collapses three very different trial models: time-limited (14/30-day), feature-limited (free tier with upgrade gates), and usage-limited (credits, seats, reports). Each has a different conversion baseline. Founders comparing against the wrong benchmark misdiagnose their performance.

5. **No "activation to upgrade" email sequence architecture.** Every guide has a drip checklist. None gives the decision logic: which events trigger which emails, what to send to an activated user vs. a dormant one, and how the upgrade email for a limit-hit user differs from the trial-expiry nudge. The sequence is event-driven, not date-driven, and no competitor covers this.

---

## Differentiating angle

Free trial to paid conversion is not one conversion event — it's a sequence of 4–6 micro-conversions, each with its own drop-off point and optimization lever. Most SaaS teams optimize the last step (the upgrade page) and ignore the four steps that determine whether the user gets there. The real conversion rate problem is rarely on the payment page; it's at activation (did the user experience value?), at habit formation (did they return?), and at the limit/gate encounter (was the upgrade prompt good enough to convert at the moment of maximum motivation?). Fix the micro-conversions in sequence and the headline conversion rate follows.
