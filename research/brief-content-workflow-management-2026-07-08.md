## Search Intent Analysis

**Who is searching**: B2B SaaS content managers, founders managing content programs, marketing leads inheriting an undocumented content operation. They've usually just had a quality problem (a bad article slipped through) or a delegation failure (a writer didn't know what to do) and are now looking for a way to systematize what they're already doing informally.

**What they want**: A practical framework they can implement immediately — not a definition piece, not a vendor pitch for a content platform.

**Funnel stage**: Top-of-funnel awareness → middle-of-funnel evaluation. Searchers at this keyword are aware they have a process problem and are researching how to solve it before deciding whether to buy software, hire someone, or build their own system.

## Competitor Analysis

**Top-ranking content profiles**: Most content at this keyword is either (a) generic project management content repurposed for content teams, or (b) vendor-owned content from Contentful, Kapost, or similar platforms pushing their tools.

**Five gaps to exploit:**

1. **No B2B SaaS-specific workflow framework** — competitor guides are written for enterprise media companies with 20-person content teams or editorial brands with print + digital pipelines. No guide addresses the 1–5-person B2B SaaS content team, which has fundamentally different constraints (async-first, no editorial layer, founder review, tight budgets).

2. **Workflow = tool setup in most guides** — the majority of content at this keyword is actually about how to set up Asana or Trello boards for content. The tool is not the workflow. No guide draws the distinction between workflow design (stages, handoffs, criteria) and workflow execution (the tool you use to track it).

3. **No handoff criteria coverage** — competitor content describes workflow stages but doesn't define what "done" looks like at each stage. Without handoff criteria, workflow documentation is useless — articles still move informally because the criteria live in the editor's head.

4. **Missing cycle time as a primary metric** — no competitor content treats cycle time (days from brief-approved to publish-ready) as the primary workflow health metric. All content focuses on output metrics (articles published, keyword rankings) rather than process metrics (where the pipeline is slow, which stage has the most variance).

5. **No scaling logic** — guides describe workflow either for "small teams" or "large teams" without specifying what changes at each scale. The content that's most useful would specify exactly what a solo operator does differently from a 3-person team from a 7-person team — and what triggers the transition.

## Differentiating Angle

**Position**: The process design article for B2B SaaS content teams — not a tool setup guide, not a general "content workflow" definition piece, not vendor content. Argues that most content quality and consistency problems are workflow problems, not writing problems. Delivers a 7-stage workflow framework with explicit handoff criteria, documentation templates, and scaling logic across three team sizes.

**Key claim**: Your content workflow already exists — it's just undocumented. The fix isn't a new tool; it's mapping what you do, adding handoff criteria at each stage, and reviewing the system quarterly.

## Article Structure

**H1**: Content workflow management: how to design a production system that doesn't break when you scale

**Key Takeaways** (5 bullets, AI-optimized for featured snippets):
1. Content workflow management is the design, documentation, and continuous improvement of stages that move content from idea to published
2. Most B2B SaaS teams work from undocumented habits — this creates quality variance and makes delegation impossible
3. An effective content workflow has 7 stages, explicit handoff criteria at each stage, and a named owner per stage
4. Cycle time (days from brief-approved to publish-ready) is the most useful single workflow health metric
5. Start with mapping what you already do; add quality gates only where variance actually causes problems

**Sections**:
1. What content workflow management actually means (vs. editorial calendar, content strategy, CMS usage)
2. The 7-stage B2B SaaS content production workflow (table: stage, input, output, quality gate, owner)
3. Designing handoffs that actually work (criteria vs. checklists; stage-completion conditions)
4. Workflow design at three team scales (solo / 3-person / 5–7 person)
5. Documenting your workflow (what to document, formats, living doc vs. frozen SOP)
6. Identifying and fixing workflow bottlenecks (cycle time measurement, three failure modes, fixes)
7. Content workflow tools (tool is not the workflow; what to look for; when to upgrade)
8. FAQ (5 questions targeting informational and commercial investigation intent)
