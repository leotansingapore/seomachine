Most B2B SaaS companies believe E-E-A-T doesn't apply to them. "We're not a health site. We don't give medical advice. E-E-A-T is a YMYL problem."

This assumption is costing them rankings.

When a VP of Engineering evaluates $80K DevOps tooling with their CTO's sign-off on the line, they're asking the same trust questions a patient asks before following medical advice — just in a different register. Do you actually understand my environment? Have your customers solved this problem at companies like mine? Can I stake my reputation on what you're claiming?

Google's E-E-A-T framework — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness — captures exactly the signals that answer those questions. The Quality Rater Guidelines explicitly apply expertise requirements to "technical content" and "software reviews," not just YMYL categories. Most B2B SaaS content fails all four E-E-A-T layers, and the rankings reflect it: thin, generic content written by non-practitioners with no author entity, no original data, and no named customer references.

E-E-A-T isn't a YMYL compliance problem. It's the trust infrastructure that determines whether your [B2B SEO strategy](/blog/b2b-seo-strategy) generates rankings that compound or stalls at page two.

This guide covers what E-E-A-T means specifically for B2B SaaS, where most companies have the biggest gaps, and how to build the four-layer trust infrastructure that ranks and converts.

> - E-E-A-T applies to B2B SaaS content because every buyer is making a high-stakes decision — the trust standard is the same as YMYL, just commercially rather than medically framed

## Why E-E-A-T matters in B2B SaaS — differently than you think

The YMYL misconception runs deep. Most B2B SaaS teams read "Your Money Your Life" and conclude: "That's health and finance. We're DevOps tooling. Doesn't apply."

Here's what the Quality Rater Guidelines actually say about technical content: pages "discussing technical topics" require demonstrated expertise from the content creator. Software review and comparison content is specifically called out as requiring expertise signals. The threshold isn't "could this advice kill someone?" — it's "is this content trying to influence a decision that matters?"

A $50K software purchase matters. A 3-year enterprise contract matters. The search results for "best project management software for engineering teams" or "SOC 2 compliance tools for B2B SaaS" are full of companies trying to influence decisions that matter — and Google's ranking systems evaluate their content accordingly.

**The B2B trust evaluation is a four-layer audit.** When a B2B buyer reads your content during the research phase of a purchase decision, they're (often unconsciously) running through:

1. **Author**: Does this person actually understand what they're writing about, or did someone run a quick internet research session and produce a content brief?
2. **Company**: Does this company have the depth and credibility I need to trust their product recommendations?
3. **Technical depth**: Is the information in this article accurate, current, and specific enough to apply to my situation?
4. **Commercial legitimacy**: Is this a company I can trust to still be around and supported in three years?

E-E-A-T is the mechanism by which your content infrastructure answers all four questions. And the failure mode for most B2B SaaS content is systematic: bylineless or generically bylined articles, no original data, assertions without citations, and no commercial trust signals anywhere near the content.

## Experience — demonstrating that you've done the work

When Google added the first "E" (Experience) in 2022, most content teams acknowledged it and moved on. It's actually the layer where B2B SaaS content is most deficient — and where the competitive advantage is most available.

Experience, for B2B SaaS content, means: **you or your customers have actually done the thing you're writing about.** Not researched it. Done it.

### Original data as the primary experience signal

"According to industry research" is an experience deficit. "Based on analysis of 400 B2B SaaS companies on our platform" is an experience signal. The difference is whether the information originates from your actual operations or from someone else's published findings.

Building an original data program:
- **Platform analytics**: aggregate anonymized product data (with appropriate customer consent) to generate benchmarks. "Companies using [feature] see a median onboarding time of X days" is uniquely authoritative because no competitor can replicate it.
- **Customer surveys**: quarterly benchmarks on 3-5 metrics that matter to your ICP. The Klaviyo Email Marketing Benchmark Report and the Intercom Customer Service Benchmark are examples — both generate enormous authority and link acquisition.
- **Buyer surveys**: annual data on how B2B buyers in your category make purchase decisions, what their biggest challenges are, how long implementations take.

This is not a one-month project. Budget 6 months to build the data infrastructure, run the first study, and publish the first report. The compounding effect on E-E-A-T — and on link acquisition — justifies the investment.

### Case study integration standards

Generic case studies ("Company X increased revenue after implementing our product") are an experience deficit. They signal that you can't get permission from customers to share specifics, which signals that the outcomes weren't compelling enough to share.

High-experience case study standards:
- **Named company**: not "a mid-market SaaS company" — "Acme Corp, a 200-person revenue operations platform"
- **Specific metrics**: not "improved performance" — "reduced sales cycle from 92 to 58 days over 6 months"
- **Mechanism explanation**: what specifically did they do with your product? Which configuration? Which process change?
- **Timeline**: when did results appear? What was the implementation timeline?

These specifics are what make content useful to a buyer evaluating whether they'll see similar results — and useful is what E-E-A-T ultimately rewards.

### SME interview protocol

Most SaaS blog content is produced without practitioner input. A content writer researches a topic, synthesizes public information, and produces something that reflects internet consensus rather than genuine depth.

The fix: build SME interviews into the editorial workflow as a standard input, not an occasional extra.

A lightweight SME protocol:
1. Writer produces an article outline and 5-7 specific questions
2. 20-minute interview with a product manager, customer success lead, or (ideally) a customer who has implemented the solution
3. Specific quotes, examples, and data points from the interview are woven into the article
4. Interviewee is credited as a contributor or reviewed the article

This adds practitioner depth that no competitor's AI-generated or research-based content can replicate.

## Expertise — author authority and topical depth

### The byline problem

Most B2B SaaS blog content has one of three author situations: no named author, a generic "Staff Writer" credit, or a junior content writer with no practitioner background in the subject.

Google's systems can infer the authority level of a byline from the author entity it can find elsewhere on the web. An author with a strong LinkedIn profile, external publications, and a consistent publication history in a domain signals expertise. An author with no findable web presence signals the absence of expertise — and the content is evaluated accordingly.

### Building author entities

An author entity is the web of signals that lets Google understand who an author is and what they're an expert in. It requires:

**Author bio pages** (mandatory): every named author needs a dedicated author page on your site with:
- Full name, photo, professional background
- Links to their LinkedIn, Twitter/X, external publications
- List of articles they've authored on your site
- Credentials or relevant experience in the subject domain

**Person schema markup**: apply structured data to author pages so Google can parse the entity directly:

```json
{
  "@type": "Person",
  "name": "Jane Smith",
  "jobTitle": "Head of Product Marketing",
  "url": "https://yoursite.com/authors/jane-smith",
  "sameAs": [
    "https://linkedin.com/in/jane-smith",
    "https://twitter.com/janesmith"
  ]
}
```

**External publication consistency**: encourage authors to publish in relevant trade press (SaaStr, G2 blog, industry publications) under the same name. Each external publication reinforces the author entity.

**Article schema markup**: every blog post should include Article or BlogPosting schema with an explicit `author` property linking to the author entity.

### Topical authority as a company-level expertise signal

Author authority is individual. Topical authority is organizational — and it's built through the comprehensiveness of your coverage on a subject.

A company that has published 15 articles on B2B content strategy — covering strategy frameworks, briefing templates, distribution channels, performance measurement, and team structure — signals expertise in a way that a company with one generic "what is content marketing" post cannot.

This is why the pillar-and-cluster architecture in your [B2B content strategy](/blog/b2b-content-strategy) and [SEO content strategy](/blog/seo-content-strategy) is also an E-E-A-T strategy: comprehensive subject coverage is how Google infers that a company understands a topic deeply.

Practical implication: before starting a new content cluster, publish at least 4-6 articles in it before expanding to new clusters. Depth signals expertise. Breadth without depth signals a content mill.

## Authoritativeness — third-party validation

Individual author expertise and company topical depth build the internal authority stack. External validation — what third parties say about you — builds authoritativeness in the E-E-A-T sense.

### The B2B authority stack

**Integration partner pages**: if your product integrates with Salesforce, HubSpot, or Slack, you should be listed in their partner ecosystems and app marketplaces. These pages are high-authority, topically relevant, and trusted by exactly your ICP — an engineer evaluating DevOps tools trusts the Datadog integration page more than a generic roundup.

**Analyst coverage**: Gartner Magic Quadrant, Forrester Wave, and G2 Grid positions are the highest-authority signals in enterprise software. Getting there requires building an analyst relations program:
- Brief major analysts quarterly (even if you're not being evaluated yet)
- Provide data proactively — analysts cite companies that make their job easier
- G2 Grid position is the entry point — cultivate G2 reviews systematically before targeting Gartner

This connects directly to the [digital PR for B2B SaaS](/blog/digital-pr-for-b2b-saas) work that builds media and analyst relationships.

**Original research as authority multiplier**: your benchmark reports, buyer surveys, and state-of-industry studies don't just create Experience signals — they become citation targets. When journalists, analysts, and other SaaS companies cite your data, you receive authoritative links from exactly the publications your ICP trusts. One well-researched original study can generate more high-quality links than six months of guest post outreach.

**Trade press editorial coverage**: earned bylines and citations in relevant trade publications (SaaStr, G2 blog, Salesforce blog, vertical industry press) build domain authority differently from generic link building. These are publications your buyer is actively reading — the links place you inside your buyer's research path. See the [link building for B2B SaaS](/blog/link-building-b2b-saas) guide for how to build trade press relationships systematically.

**Customer advocacy links**: when a named customer company writes about their implementation of your product on their own blog (a "tools we use" post, a detailed case study), the resulting link is the highest-quality authority signal available. It's from a real company, in your buyer's network, with a genuine endorsement. Build a customer advocacy program that makes this easy and incentivizes it.

## Trustworthiness — technical and commercial trust architecture

Trustworthiness in E-E-A-T has three sub-dimensions for B2B SaaS: technical trust, commercial trust, and content trust.

### Technical trust signals

**Security and compliance visibility**: in enterprise B2B, security concerns are often the primary gating factor in vendor evaluation. SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, GDPR compliance, HIPAA (if health-adjacent), and FedRAMP (if government) certifications need to be:
- Prominently displayed on your security/trust page
- Linked from your pricing page, product pages, and footer
- Accompanied by downloadable documentation (pen test reports, security questionnaire templates)

Burying compliance in a footer link signals that it's not something you lead with. Leading with it signals confidence.

**Performance and Core Web Vitals**: a slow, technically broken site erodes trust in a way that's not rational but is real. An enterprise buyer who hits a 5-second page load or a broken mobile layout subconsciously infers that if you can't maintain your own site, you probably can't maintain their infrastructure either. A comprehensive [technical SEO audit](/blog/technical-seo-audit) that addresses Core Web Vitals is also an E-E-A-T investment.

**Privacy policy written for humans**: cookie-cutter legalese privacy policies signal that you don't want buyers to understand what you do with their data. A plain-language, well-organized privacy policy signals transparency.

### Commercial trust signals

**Named customer references**: "500+ companies trust us" is an authority placeholder. "Figma, Notion, and Intercom trust us for X" is a trust signal. Named logos with named contacts available for reference are the commercial trust equivalent of an author entity — they're verifiable, specific, and impossible to fake.

**Transparent pricing or clear custom-pricing explanation**: enterprise SaaS companies often hide pricing for legitimate commercial reasons. The trust issue is when they hide it without explanation. "Pricing is based on seat count and contract term — our smallest plan starts at $X/month. [Book a demo]" is transparent. "Contact us for pricing" with no context is a trust deficit.

**Review volume and recency on G2/Capterra**: a B2B SaaS company with 12 G2 reviews, the most recent from 18 months ago, is signaling product stagnation. A company with 200 recent reviews, averaging 4.5 stars, is signaling customer satisfaction and commercial momentum. Cultivate reviews systematically: post-onboarding NPS triggers, customer success team asks at QBRs, review incentive programs.

### Content trust signals

**Last-updated dates displayed and maintained**: a guide that was accurate in 2022 may be actively misleading in 2026. Displaying the last-updated date — and actually updating content when it's stale — is both an E-E-A-T signal and a reader respect signal.

**External citations for factual claims**: every statistic, benchmark, and research finding should link to the original source. "Studies show..." with no link is an assertion, not evidence. "A 2024 Salesforce State of Sales report found..." with a link to the source is a trust signal.

**No unsubstantiated superlatives**: "industry-leading," "best-in-class," and "world-class" are trust deficits. They signal that you have no specific evidence for the claim and are filling space with adjectives. Replace every superlative with a specific claim: instead of "industry-leading support," try "median first-response time of 4 minutes, 24/7."

## E-E-A-T content audit — where to start

Auditing before fixing prevents wasted effort. A 100-post blog needs triage, not a blanket upgrade. Here's a seven-step process to identify the highest-leverage opportunities.

**Step 1**: Export all blog posts from GA4 (Organic channel, Last 90 days) sorted by sessions, descending.

**Step 2**: Identify the top 30 pages by organic sessions. These are the pages where E-E-A-T upgrades have the highest traffic impact.

**Step 3 — Author entity check**: Does each page have a named author? Does that author have a bio page with external links? Does the page include Article schema with an author property?

**Step 4 — Experience check**: Does each page include original data, named case studies with specific metrics, or expert quotes from practitioners — or does it only cite third-party research?

**Step 5 — Trust check**: Does each page cite external sources for factual claims? Is the last-updated date displayed? Does the page avoid unsubstantiated superlatives?

**Step 6 — Schema check**: Does each page have Article or BlogPosting schema? FAQ schema where applicable? Author property with a link to the author entity?

**Step 7 — Competitive check**: For pages ranking in positions 5-15, check whether competitors ranking above you have stronger author entities, more original data, or more external citations. Identify the specific gap.

**Prioritization**: focus first on pages that rank positions 5-15 (most to gain) with high existing traffic (most impact). Quick wins: add author bios and Article schema across all top pages. Longer-term: build original data program and upgrade case study library.

## Measuring E-E-A-T progress

E-E-A-T is an inference Google makes from dozens of signals, not a direct metric. What you can measure:

| Signal | Proxy metric | Tool |
|---|---|---|
| Author entity recognition | Author name appears in Knowledge Panel | Google Search |
| Branded search growth | Volume trend for brand + author name queries | GSC (branded filters) |
| Backlink quality | Average domain authority of linking domains | Ahrefs / Semrush |
| Review volume and recency | G2/Capterra review count and most recent date | G2 / Capterra dashboard |
| Schema completeness | % of blog posts with Article + Author schema | Screaming Frog |
| Ranking trend | Position change for target keywords | GSC + rank tracker |
| Engagement depth | Average engagement time per page | GA4 |

Track these metrics monthly. E-E-A-T improvements typically manifest in rankings over a 3-6 month window — the feedback loop is slower than on-page keyword optimization because you're building infrastructure signals, not just adjusting copy.

Set up the full measurement framework using the [SEO reporting](/blog/seo-reporting) process — tracking both ranking signals and engagement signals together gives you the clearest read on whether your E-E-A-T work is landing.

## The E-E-A-T build sequence for B2B SaaS

If you're starting from a low E-E-A-T baseline, prioritize in this order:

**Month 1-2 (infrastructure)**:
- Author bio pages for every named author
- Article and Person schema markup across all blog content
- Last-updated dates added to all articles
- External citations added to all factual claims in top 30 posts

**Month 3-4 (authority building)**:
- SME interview protocol established and running
- G2 review cultivation program launched (post-onboarding trigger + CS asks)
- Original data study scoped and underway (first publication target: 6 months out)
- Trade press editorial program started (one contributed byline per month)

**Month 5-6 (depth expansion)**:
- First original data study published
- Case study library upgraded with specific metrics and named companies
- Analyst relations outreach started (begin with G2, then work toward Gartner)
- Integration partner pages pursued through partner programs

**Month 7+ (compounding)**:
- Original research generates link acquisition
- Author entities gain recognition
- Review volume compounds (G2 Grid position improves)
- Ranking improvements manifest and compound
