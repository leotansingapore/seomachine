Most link prospecting guides are built for e-commerce sites and content publishers. They tell you to run the Ahrefs Link Intersect tool, find broken links, and send cold emails until something sticks. That workflow does work -- for certain site types. For SaaS products, it misses half the prospect universe entirely.

SaaS companies have a link acquisition source that e-commerce brands don't: a product that generates links through directories, integration marketplaces, and review platforms without a single cold outreach email. G2 links to your product page whether you outreach for it or not. Zapier lists every integration partner at a domain rating above 90. Your tech stack partners have "works with" pages that include you automatically when you join their partner program.

This guide covers both tracks: the outreach-based prospecting every guide covers, and the product-led passive sources that most SaaS teams overlook. Together they produce 50+ qualified prospects per month with roughly two hours of prospecting time per week.

**Key Takeaways**:
- Link prospecting for SaaS is a two-track system: active (outreach-based) and passive (product-led)
- Passive sources -- review sites, integration marketplaces, partner pages -- require no cold outreach and are underused by most SaaS teams
- Qualify every prospect against 4 criteria before investing outreach time: relevance, editorial status, traffic, and link type
- 2 hours of prospecting per week produces 50+ qualified prospects per month
- The prospecting stack (Ahrefs + Hunter + Google Sheets) is sufficient for most SaaS teams doing fewer than 200 outreach emails per month

## Why SaaS link prospecting is different from generic link building

Generic link building advice optimizes for domain rating and volume. Get links from high-DA sites. Build a lot of them. For consumer content sites and e-commerce, that framing is largely correct.

For B2B SaaS, the buyer-journey dimension matters more. A link from a niche SaaS newsletter read by 8,000 VP of Sales readers outperforms a link from a generic DR70 content site read by nobody who buys software. A mention in a Shopify App Store listing connects you to a verified purchase-intent audience. A link from a product review on G2 sits in the research phase of a multi-month buying cycle.

The practical implication: relevance and audience quality are the primary qualification criteria for SaaS prospects, not DR alone.

The other difference is that SaaS products have passive link sources that e-commerce brands lack. A shoe brand cannot list itself on Zapier. A CRM can -- and should. That asymmetry creates a significant prospecting advantage for software companies willing to build it systematically.

A realistic monthly target for a SaaS team doing link building in-house: 50 qualified prospects. Of those, 10-15 will respond to outreach. Of those, 4-6 links will close. That's a pipeline, not a lottery.

## Track 1: Active prospecting (outreach-based)

Active prospecting means finding sites where you would pitch yourself: for a guest post, a backlink addition, a resource page inclusion, or a content collaboration. This is the track that most link prospecting guides cover.

### Content site prospecting via Link Intersect

Ahrefs Link Intersect shows you which sites link to your competitors but not to you. These are the highest-probability prospects: they already link to SaaS content in your category and have established they're willing to include external links.

Workflow:
1. Open Ahrefs > Link Intersect
2. Enter 3-5 direct competitors (not tangential SaaS products -- exact category competitors)
3. Filter: DR 30+, traffic 1,000+ visits/month (Ahrefs estimate), "dofollow" link type
4. Export the output (typically 200-500 results for an established category)
5. Qualify the export: remove review sites and directories (those go in Track 2), remove obvious PBNs, remove sites with no real editorial voice

What you're left with is the content site universe that considers your category worth linking to. These are your best-fit outreach targets.

### Guest post prospecting via search operators

Guest posting works when you find sites that (a) publish contributed content, (b) have a real editorial standard, and (c) have an audience that includes your buyers.

Search operators for finding prospects:
- `"write for us" + [SaaS vertical]` -- e.g., "write for us" + marketing automation
- `"contributor guidelines" + [audience type]` -- e.g., "contributor guidelines" + SaaS founders
- `"guest post" + [topic]` -- e.g., "guest post" + B2B sales
- `intitle:"submit a post" [topic]`

Qualification check before adding to your list: Does the site have a real editorial process (not just accepting any post with a dofollow link)? Does the author bio include a link? Is there traffic behind it (Ahrefs traffic > 500/mo)? Would your target buyer actually read this publication?

Skip any site that accepts posts without editorial review, has no traffic, or exists primarily to sell links. The link won't help you and may hurt you.

### Resource page prospecting

Resource pages -- curated lists of tools, guides, and recommended software -- are often overlooked but highly effective. A link from a "Best CRM tools for startups" resource page is contextually perfect for a CRM product.

Find them with: `inurl:resources "best [category] tools"` or `intitle:"tools for [audience]"`.

The pitch is simple: you're not asking to replace anyone on the list, you're offering to be added. Keep the email to three sentences. Demonstrate you read the page.

### Podcast and community prospecting

Podcast guest appearances generate links from show notes -- and show notes pages often have real traffic and domain authority behind them. The host links to your site, your products, and your social profiles as a matter of format.

Find SaaS-relevant podcasts via Spotify, Apple Podcasts (search your category), or the Podcast Guest Database. Qualify: is the host linking to past guests? Is the audience your buyer profile?

Community prospecting means finding threads, AMAs, and high-visibility posts in places like Hacker News "Show HN", Indie Hackers, and relevant subreddits where a genuine contribution (not spam) can earn referral links from content creators who cite you later. Track these as brand mentions, not link prospects -- they're lower-certainty but require zero outreach cost.

## Track 2: Passive prospecting (product-led)

Passive prospecting means finding places your product should be listed, claimed, and optimized -- where the link comes with the listing itself, not from a cold email. Most SaaS teams have not fully claimed their passive link universe.

### SaaS review sites and directories

Every major SaaS review platform links to your product with a contextual, dofollow (or valuable nofollow) link from a high-DA domain. These are not "directory submissions" in the old SEO sense -- they're platforms that real buyers use to evaluate software.

The core list:
- **G2** (DR 91) -- most authoritative; buyer reviews drive referral traffic
- **Capterra** (DR 89) -- strong for SMB and mid-market buyers
- **GetApp** (DR 82) -- Capterra's sister platform; separate listing required
- **Software Advice** (DR 78) -- Capterra network, different audience segment
- **TrustRadius** (DR 79) -- strong for enterprise and B2B
- **Product Hunt** (DR 90) -- launch page earns a permanent DR90 link on your launch date; profile link is permanent
- **AlternativeTo** (DR 84) -- "alternatives to [competitor]" queries land here; being listed as an alternative to your category leaders is high-intent traffic
- **SaaSHub** (DR 72) -- aggregator that also tracks pricing changes and reviews
- **Crozdesk** (DR 65) -- niche software discovery platform

Prospecting action: run your company name through each platform. Claim any unclaimed listing. Optimize the profile page (product description, screenshots, categories) -- a well-optimized listing earns more clicks and more reviews, which compounds the traffic signal Google uses to evaluate the page's quality.

### Integration marketplace listings

Integration marketplaces are the most underused link source in SaaS. Each listing is a contextual, DR 70-90 link from a platform your buyers already use.

The core integration marketplaces:
- **Zapier** (DR 91) -- every integration is listed at zapier.com/apps/[your-product]
- **Make (formerly Integromat)** (DR 77) -- similar structure to Zapier
- **HubSpot App Marketplace** (DR 92) -- extremely high-intent; HubSpot users actively searching for compatible tools
- **Salesforce AppExchange** (DR 91) -- enterprise buyers; high trust signal
- **Shopify App Store** (DR 91) -- relevant if you serve e-commerce businesses
- **Slack App Directory** (DR 96) -- workplace tool integrations
- **Microsoft AppSource** (DR 93) -- Microsoft ecosystem; strong enterprise signal
- **Pipedrive Marketplace** (DR 78), **Intercom App Store** (DR 83), **Notion Integrations** (DR 91)

Prospecting action: list every integration you have already built. Then look at your competitor listings on Zapier and AppExchange -- any marketplace where they appear that you don't is a passive link gap. Building a new integration specifically to earn a marketplace listing is a legitimate link building strategy when the integration serves your users.

### Partner and technology partner pages

Most SaaS companies have technology partners -- their CRM, their payment processor, their analytics stack, their CDP. Many of those partners maintain "technology ecosystem" or "works with" pages that link to certified partners.

Prospecting workflow:
1. List every software product in your tech stack
2. For each, search: `site:[partner-domain].com "technology partners"` or `"works with" [partner product name]`
3. Identify which ones have a formal partner program with a public directory
4. Apply to those programs; request listing on partner pages

This is low-volume but high-authority. A link from your CRM vendor's partner directory is contextually exact and carries trust from a domain your buyers already use.

## How to qualify a prospect before outreach

Unqualified prospecting wastes more time than it saves. Before adding a site to your outreach list, run the 4-filter check:

**1. Relevance to SaaS buyer**: Would your target buyer encounter this site during their research process? If the answer is "probably not," the link won't drive referral traffic and provides limited topical authority signal for your vertical.

**2. Editorial vs. sponsored**: Does the site publish editorial links (links placed because of content quality) or is it primarily a link sales operation? Check: do they have a "sponsored post" or "advertiser disclosure" label on every post? Does their content exist primarily as a link vehicle? Skip these -- they're risky.

**3. Traffic signal**: Ahrefs estimated traffic above 500 visits/month is a basic threshold. No traffic usually means no editorial authority. Check the traffic trend -- declining sites aren't worth outreach investment even at high DR.

**4. Link type**: Is the site likely to give you a dofollow editorial link? Review sites and directories often use nofollow or sponsored link attributes. That's fine for brand visibility and referral traffic, but factor it in when prioritizing outreach effort.

A prospect that passes all four filters is qualified. One that passes three of four is worth reviewing. One that fails relevance or editorial is off the list.

## The prospecting stack

You don't need enterprise tooling for 50 prospects per month.

- **Ahrefs** -- Link Intersect, Content Explorer (for finding resource pages), Site Explorer (for competitor link profiles). This is the primary prospecting tool.
- **Hunter.io** -- email lookup for outreach contacts. Enter a domain, get the editorial contacts. Sufficient for most SaaS prospecting lists.
- **Apollo.io** -- firmographic enrichment for larger outreach programs (200+ contacts). Overkill for under 50 prospects/month.
- **Google Sheets** -- prospect tracking. Simpler than a CRM for teams doing under 200 outreach emails/month.
- **Pitchbox or BuzzStream** -- optional. Add these when manual email management becomes a bottleneck (typically above 200 outreach sequences/month running simultaneously).

Workflow in sequence: Export from Ahrefs → qualify in Sheets using the 4-filter check → enrich contact info with Hunter → load into outreach sequence.

## Building to 50 qualified prospects per month

Fifty qualified prospects per month requires roughly 8 hours of work -- two hours per week. Here's how to distribute it:

**Week 1**: Run Link Intersect for 3 direct competitors. Export results. Apply the 4-filter qualification to the list. Target: 20-25 qualified content site prospects.

**Week 2**: Guest post sweep using search operators for 2-3 relevant publications. Qualify and add. Sweep your community channels (Reddit, Indie Hackers, HN) for contextual opportunities. Target: 10-15 qualified prospects.

**Week 3**: Passive source audit. Check every integration marketplace: are you listed everywhere your competitors are listed? Any new marketplaces to claim? Check all review platforms: any unclaimed listings? Target: 5-10 new passive link assets.

**Week 4**: Resource page sweep for any new "best [category]" roundups published in the past 30 days (Content Explorer, date filter: last 30 days). Target: 5-8 resource page prospects.

Track your prospects in a Sheets table with: prospect_date, source_type, domain, DR, Ahrefs traffic, relevance_score (1-3), outreach_status, link_closed (Y/N). Review the pipeline monthly. Any domain you've outreached to twice with no response is deprioritized for 90 days.

## FAQ

**How many prospects do I need to get 5 links per month?**

Expect a 10-15% close rate on outreach to qualified prospects. To close 5 links, you need 35-50 qualified prospects who you actually contact. A list of 50 qualified prospects doesn't mean 50 outreach emails -- some won't have contact information, some will be low priority. Plan for 35 actual outreach emails per month to close 4-6 links.

**Should I prospect manually or use a tool like Pitchbox?**

Manual prospecting (Ahrefs + Sheets + Hunter) is sufficient for most SaaS teams under 200 outreach emails/month. Pitchbox and BuzzStream add automation for follow-up sequences and relationship tracking -- genuinely useful once your program scales, but unnecessary overhead before that point.

**What's the difference between a link prospect and a link target?**

A prospect is a site you've identified as potentially worth pursuing. A target is a site you've qualified and decided to outreach to. The prospecting phase produces a large list of prospects; the qualification step cuts it down to a smaller list of targets. Conflating the two leads to wasted outreach effort.

**How do I find prospects when my SaaS is in a very niche industry?**

Niche SaaS products often have a smaller content site ecosystem but a richer passive link universe. Integration marketplaces, vertical-specific SaaS directories, and industry association partner pages can provide more links than cold outreach in niche verticals. Also: your competitors in that niche have almost certainly not done a systematic Link Intersect audit. Any links they've earned from trade publications and vertical-specific resources are likely available to you with the right pitch.
