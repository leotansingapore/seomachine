# Content Gap Analysis: How to Find the Keywords Your Competitors Own

A content gap analysis is the process of identifying keywords your competitors rank for that your site does not -- then building content to close those gaps and capture that traffic. Run systematically, it turns your competitors' content library into your keyword roadmap.

Most keyword research starts from scratch. Content gap analysis starts from proof: your competitors are already ranking for these terms. The demand is confirmed. The gap is documented. All that's missing is the content.

> **Key Takeaways**
> - A content gap analysis finds keywords competitors rank for that you don't -- giving you a pre-validated list of content opportunities
> - There are 3 gap types: keyword gaps (topics you haven't covered), position gaps (topics where competitors outrank you), and intent gaps (topics where your content doesn't match what searchers want)
> - The average mid-size website has 150-400 keyword gaps against its top 3 competitors (Semrush)
> - Not all gaps are worth closing -- qualify each gap by search intent, volume, and keyword difficulty before building content
> - The best opportunities combine medium search volume, manageable difficulty, and clear commercial follow-through

## What is a content gap analysis

A content gap analysis compares your organic keyword footprint against your competitors'. The result is a list of specific search terms your competitors appear for in Google that your site does not.

These gaps represent topics your audience is actively searching, that your competitors are already answering, and that your content library is missing. Every gap is a page you haven't written yet that a competitor is using to intercept potential customers who should be finding you.

There are three gap types, and each requires a different fix:

**Keyword gaps**: Terms your competitors rank for (any position) that you have zero rankings for. These are completely uncovered topics -- new content required.

**Position gaps**: Terms where both you and a competitor rank, but the competitor holds a significantly stronger position. They're on page 1; you're buried on page 3. The topic is covered; the content needs to be improved.

**Intent gaps**: Terms where you're technically covering the topic but your content doesn't match what searchers are actually looking for. You rank for the informational version of a query while a competitor captures the commercial version. Fixing this usually means a new page, not just on-page edits.

Identifying which gap type you're dealing with before you act saves significant production time.

## Why content gap analysis beats traditional keyword research

Standard keyword research starts with a blank slate. You brainstorm topics, run them through a tool, check volume and difficulty, and build a content calendar from statistical projections.

Content gap analysis inverts the process. Your competitors have already done the research and built the content. Competitors who've been in your niche for years don't sustain investment in content that doesn't perform. When you analyze their keyword footprint, you're looking at a tested map -- not a projection.

Marcus ran marketing at a logistics SaaS startup in 2024. His team published 20 articles over 8 months covering supply chain trends, warehouse management, and fulfillment cost calculators. The content performed reasonably well on the topics it covered.

Then his agency ran a content gap analysis against his top 3 competitors. The analysis surfaced 214 keyword gaps. The largest miss: "freight tracking software" at 2,800 searches per month. One competitor's comparison page for that keyword was pulling an estimated 1,100 organic visits per month. Marcus's site had zero visibility for the term -- despite his product literally tracking freight.

None of his team's keyword brainstorming sessions had surfaced that phrase. The competitors' content had.

The gap analysis gave Marcus's team a ready-to-execute list of 40 prioritized content opportunities. They shipped 12 in the following quarter. Organic traffic from those 12 pages hit 2,300 visits in month three.

## How to run a content gap analysis (5 steps)

### Step 1: Identify your real SEO competitors

Your SEO competitors are not the same as your business competitors. For this analysis, you want sites that rank for the keywords your audience searches -- not just companies selling a similar product.

Run your 5-10 target keywords into Google. The sites that consistently appear on page 1 are your SEO competitors. A DA 65 industry blog ranking for 40 of your target keywords is more relevant to this analysis than a venture-funded startup with 8 blog posts.

Pick 3-5 competitors. Fewer than 3 misses gaps; more than 5 adds noise.

For each, note:
- Their domain
- Their content focus (blog-heavy? comparison pages? tool-based content?)
- Their estimated organic traffic (Ahrefs Site Explorer or Semrush gives a rough figure)

### Step 2: Pull their ranking keywords

Use a keyword research tool to export your competitors' organic keyword rankings. Every major tool supports this directly:

- **Ahrefs**: Site Explorer > enter competitor domain > Organic keywords report
- **Semrush**: Domain Overview > Organic Research > Positions
- **Moz Pro**: Keyword Explorer > Competitive Analysis

For each competitor, export their top 500-1,000 ranking keywords. Filter out branded terms -- competitor product names, founder names, company-specific terms. You're looking for topical keywords that represent content opportunities, not their own brand searches.

Export to a spreadsheet. You'll need this in step 3.

### Step 3: Find the gaps

Pull your own site's ranking keywords using the same tool. Most tools let you do this against your verified domain.

Now compare your list against the competitor lists. You're looking for terms that appear in competitor exports but don't appear in your own rankings at all -- or where you rank below position 30.

Every major tool has a gap feature built in:
- **Ahrefs**: Content Gap tool under Competitive Analysis
- **Semrush**: Keyword Gap tool
- **Moz Pro**: True Competitor + Keyword Gap

Input up to 5 competitor domains and your own. The output is a filtered list of terms competitors rank for that your site doesn't. This is your raw gap list.

A typical analysis against 3 competitors surfaces 150-600 raw gaps. Step 4 cuts that to the 20-30 that are actually worth pursuing.

### Step 4: Qualify gaps by intent, volume, and difficulty

Apply three filters to your raw gap list:

**Volume filter**: Set a floor. For most B2B sites, 200+ monthly searches is a reasonable minimum. Below that, the traffic return rarely justifies content production cost -- especially early in the campaign when you're still building domain authority.

**Difficulty filter**: Prioritize gaps with keyword difficulty under 50. High-difficulty gaps against established competitors with DA 80+ are winnable eventually, but require real link equity behind the content. For mid-authority domains, start with medium-difficulty gaps and build toward the harder targets over 12-18 months.

**Intent filter**: Match each gap to your business model and funnel stage. Informational keywords build awareness; commercial keywords capture buyers who are actively comparing options. Flag each gap as high-intent (purchase-ready), medium-intent (comparing), or low-intent (researching broadly).

After filtering, most raw lists compress to 25-50 actionable opportunities.

Rank your shortlist using this formula:

**Priority score = (Monthly volume x 0.4) + ((100 - KD) x 0.3) + (Commercial intent score x 0.3)**

Assign commercial intent scores: 10 for high (buyer-ready), 6 for medium (comparing options), 2 for low (broadly informational).

The top 10 results of that ranking are your first quarter's work.

### Step 5: Build your gap-closing content plan

Your qualified gap list is a content brief queue. For each prioritized gap:

**Check the SERP before writing.** Search the term in Google. Look at what content types rank: listicles, step-by-step guides, tool comparison pages, or video results. Match the format to what Google rewards for that specific query. A how-to guide published against a SERP full of comparison pages will miss the intent signal regardless of content quality.

**Assess the competition on page 1.** Note word counts, how comprehensively they cover the topic, and whether any results are thin or visibly outdated. Outdated content on page 1 is your clearest entry point -- a more thorough, current article can displace it.

**Map to your cluster structure.** A gap analysis output connects to your [SEO content strategy](/blog/seo-content-strategy) at the planning layer. Each gap should attach to an existing cluster pillar or seed a new one. Isolated articles without cluster context are harder to rank and harder to build internal link equity through.

**Write a brief and add to pipeline.** Assign the gap term as primary keyword, pull the top 3 competing articles as structural reference points, set a word count target based on what page 1 averages, and add to your content calendar with an assigned owner and deadline. A gap analysis without execution ownership is a spreadsheet, not a strategy.

## Content gap analysis tools

**Ahrefs Content Gap** (paid, $99+/mo)
Best-in-class for this workflow. Input up to 10 competitor domains and your own, filter by keyword position, volume, and difficulty, and get a clean gap list with position data for each competitor across the full keyword set. The most accurate keyword database available.

**Semrush Keyword Gap** (paid, $120+/mo)
Comparable to Ahrefs in depth. The Keyword Gap tool shows gaps filtered by keyword type -- organic, paid, and product listing ads. Useful if you're running paid campaigns alongside organic and want to identify gaps across both channels simultaneously.

**Google Search Console + spreadsheet** (free)
Not a purpose-built gap tool, but functional for position gaps on your own site. Export all queries from GSC, filter for positions 11-30, and cross-reference against competitor top pages from Ahrefs or Semrush free tiers. Slower than a dedicated tool; workable for teams without a paid tool budget.

**Moz Pro Keyword Gap** (paid, $100+/mo)
Solid US-focused data with a cleaner UX than Ahrefs for teams new to competitive analysis. The keyword database is slightly less comprehensive than Ahrefs or Semrush, but the gap reports are easier to interpret for stakeholders unfamiliar with keyword research tools.

## Common mistakes

**Chasing volume without checking intent.** A 10,000/mo keyword in your gap list isn't automatically worth pursuing. If the SERP is dominated by enterprise vendors with 10x your domain authority, and the query is purely informational with no commercial follow-through, it's a traffic play -- not a revenue play. Filter for intent and realistic winability before committing production resources.

**Skipping the SERP check.** The gap tool tells you competitors rank for a keyword. It doesn't tell you what kind of content Google rewards for that query. Run every prioritized gap through a Google search before writing. If the top 5 results are tool comparison pages and you're planning a how-to guide, you'll miss the intent signal entirely -- and the ranking.

**Trying to close 100 gaps at once.** A gap analysis producing 400 keyword opportunities doesn't mean you need 400 pieces of content. It means you have 400 data points to filter. Twenty well-executed articles outperform 80 thin ones in every competitive niche. Apply the priority formula and work the top 10-20 opportunities first.

**Not revisiting after publishing.** Run the gap analysis again 90 days after publishing content. Some gaps will close; others won't move despite solid content. The articles that don't gain traction usually need a link-building push or an on-page optimization pass -- the [on-page SEO checklist](/blog/on-page-seo-checklist) covers the specific checks for articles that have stalled after publishing.

## FAQ

**How often should I run a content gap analysis?**
Every 6 months as a baseline. Run it again any time a competitor launches a content push (their keyword footprint will expand quickly), or when you add a new product or service category that changes your target keyword set.

**How is a content gap analysis different from keyword research?**
Keyword research builds a list from intent and volume projections. Content gap analysis builds a list from competitor evidence. Both end at the same output -- a prioritized content plan -- but gap analysis is faster to validate because you're not estimating demand, you're measuring it from competitors who have already confirmed the market exists.

**Can I run a content gap analysis without paid tools?**
Yes, but it's slower. Google Search Console shows your own rankings. Combine that with a manually built competitor keyword list using Ahrefs' free Webmaster Tools (for your own site) and Semrush's free tier (limited competitor data). The free approach covers the basics; paid tools give you competitor data at scale with less manual assembly.

**What if my top gaps require DA 80+ to rank for?**
Skip them for now. Add them to a watch list and revisit in 12-18 months after you've built domain authority through a systematic [keyword research-backed content strategy](/blog/keyword-research-for-seo). Prioritizing gaps you can't realistically win wastes production budget that would generate better returns on medium-difficulty targets.

**How many gaps should I target per quarter?**
For most content teams, 10-15 pieces per quarter is a realistic target. At that cadence, a well-prioritized gap list of 40-50 items gives you roughly 3-4 quarters of planned content -- long enough to see compounding returns from the earliest pieces before you need to run another analysis.

## What to do next

The analysis is only useful if the content gets written.

The common failure mode: a thorough gap analysis gets run, a detailed spreadsheet gets produced, the team reviews it in a meeting -- and six months later, the spreadsheet sits in a shared drive untouched because nobody owns the execution.

Assign ownership before the analysis is finished. Every prioritized gap needs a keyword brief, a deadline, and a writer. Without that, gap analysis is research theater.
