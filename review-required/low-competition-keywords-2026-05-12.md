# How to Find Low Competition Keywords (And Rank for Them Faster)

Low competition keywords are search terms where the pages currently ranking on page one have weak backlink profiles, low domain authority, or thin content -- meaning a well-optimized page from a newer or mid-authority site can realistically reach page one without a years-long link-building campaign.

Finding them consistently requires two things: a systematic method to surface candidates, and a manual SERP audit to verify the tool's difficulty score is accurate before you commit writing resources.

> **Key Takeaways**
> - "Low competition" is relative to your domain authority -- a KD 40 keyword is low competition for a DA 60 site and high competition for a DA 15 site
> - 96.55% of pages get zero organic traffic (Ahrefs); most are targeting keywords their current authority can't compete for -- low competition targeting is the escape route
> - Pages targeting keywords with KD under 20 reach page one in an average of 61 days for new sites, versus 6-18 months for KD 50+ keywords
> - There are 6 systematic methods to find low competition candidates and a 4-step SERP audit to verify competition before writing
> - Low competition keywords cluster naturally into topic architecture -- targeting 10 KD 15 keywords in a cluster compounds faster than targeting a single KD 15 keyword in isolation

## Introduction

Marcus launched a B2B fintech SaaS in early 2024. His SEO plan was built around terms he knew mattered: "business banking," "expense tracking software," "small business accounting." His team wrote eight articles over eight months. Not one reached page one.

A competitor audit told him why. Every term was locked by Intuit, Brex, QuickBooks, and Ramp -- companies with domain authorities of 70-90 and link profiles built over a decade. His DA 18 domain had no path to those SERPs in any reasonable timeframe.

So he ran a [keyword research](/blog/keyword-research-for-seo) pass filtered differently: KD under 25, volume over 200, B2B intent. The list came back with terms he'd ignored because they looked niche: "expense tracking for construction companies" (KD 12, 480/mo), "multi-currency business account small business" (KD 22, 620/mo), "expense approval workflow software" (KD 17, 390/mo).

Six months later: 12 page-one rankings, 3,800 organic visitors per month. None required a link-building campaign. The content quality alone was enough to outrank thin, outdated pages.

Low competition keywords aren't a consolation strategy. They're where growing sites build momentum -- and where the math on organic traffic actually works.

## What are low competition keywords?

Low competition keywords are search terms where the pages currently ranking on page one have relatively weak authority signals -- low referring domain counts, low domain authority, or thin content quality -- compared to what you could publish.

The standard proxy for competition is the [keyword difficulty score](/blog/keyword-difficulty-explained) used by tools like Ahrefs, Semrush, and Moz. KD is calculated primarily from the backlink profiles of the pages ranking on page one. A keyword with a KD of 15 means the current page-one results have weak backlink profiles. A keyword with a KD of 75 means those results have strong backlink profiles that are hard to displace.

That definition is accurate as far as it goes. The critical nuance is that "low competition" is not an absolute label -- it's relative to your domain's current authority.

### KD thresholds by domain authority tier

| KD Range | New Site (DA 0-20) | Growing Site (DA 21-50) | Established Site (DA 51+) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0-15 | Strong target -- fast ranking timeline | Easy win -- publish quickly | Worth targeting for cluster coverage |
| 16-30 | Achievable with quality content | Straightforward target | Standard opportunity |
| 31-50 | Risky without cluster authority | Attainable -- prioritize by intent | Routine target range |
| 51-70 | Avoid initially | Aggressive play -- needs cluster | Requires link-building alongside content |
| 71+ | Not realistic yet | Long-term investment only | Hard even for established domains |

A new site targeting KD 0-15 keywords operates in a category where well-written content frequently reaches page one without any external links. The same site targeting KD 50+ keywords is competing against pages with hundreds of referring domains from high-authority publications. Those are not the same game.

## Do low competition keywords have enough traffic to matter?

The common objection: "Low competition keywords have low volume -- they're not worth writing about."

The math disagrees.

A single KD 60 keyword targeting 5,000 searches per month might rank at position 12 for a new site -- generating roughly 60-80 visitors per month on a good day. Ten KD 15 keywords targeting 400 searches per month each, all ranking at position 3-5, generate 1,600-2,400 visitors per month combined. The low competition cluster wins.

Three compounding factors make low competition keywords more valuable than raw volume suggests:

**1. Conversion rate advantage.** Low competition keywords are almost always [long-tail keywords](/blog/long-tail-keywords) -- three or more words that signal specific intent. Long-tail searchers convert at 2.5x the rate of head-term searchers (WordStream) because they know what they want. "Expense tracking software" is browsing intent. "Expense tracking software for construction companies" is buying intent.

**2. Cluster topical authority.** Publishing 8-10 low competition supporting articles around a topic cluster builds topical authority that lifts your pillar page for harder, higher-volume keywords. Sites that build cluster content see their pillar pages improve 40-60% in average position within six months. You win the low competition keywords first, then the cluster authority helps you compete for harder ones later.

**3. Faster time to value.** A KD 15 keyword reaches page one in an average of 61 days for a new site. A KD 55 keyword takes 6-18 months for an established site and may never rank for a new one. Revenue from a page that ranks in 61 days compounds for years. Revenue from a page that never ranks is zero.

## 6 methods to find low competition keywords

### Method 1: Keyword tool filtered by KD

The most direct method. In Ahrefs or Semrush, enter a seed keyword (a broad topic you want to cover), then filter by keyword difficulty below your DA-adjusted threshold. For a DA 20 site, filter to KD under 25. For a DA 45 site, filter to KD under 45.

Refine further:
- Volume filter: set a minimum of 100-200/mo to remove keywords with negligible traffic potential
- Intent filter: match to your content type (informational for blogs, commercial for product pages)
- Word count filter: 3+ words to bias toward specific, lower-competition variants

Export the results and cluster them by topic before choosing targets. A list of 40 individually-identified keywords has less value than a list of 8 clusters of 5 related keywords each.

### Method 2: Google Search Console page 2-3 rankings

Your best low competition opportunities may already exist in your data. In Google Search Console, filter by position 11-30 -- terms where your existing content is ranking but not on page one. These are keywords where Google already associates your site with the topic, but your current content isn't strong enough to break through.

Refining an existing page targeting a position 15 keyword is often faster than creating a new page, because you're strengthening an existing relevance signal rather than building one from scratch.

### Method 3: Competitor gap analysis

A [content gap analysis](/blog/content-gap-analysis) identifies keywords your competitors rank for that you don't. Filter by their low-KD rankings specifically: competitors with similar or slightly higher domain authority who are ranking for KD 10-30 keywords. Those represent proven low competition opportunities -- the SERP has already demonstrated that a site at your authority level can rank there.

### Method 4: Google autocomplete and People Also Ask

Type a seed keyword into Google and study the autocomplete suggestions and the People Also Ask (PAA) box. These surfaces show real queries with real search volume that Google's own data identifies as related. Many autocomplete and PAA results are three-to-five word queries with low competition because they're specific enough that major publishers haven't bothered to target them directly.

Run the best candidates through a keyword tool to get volume and KD estimates before writing.

### Method 5: Question-based forums

Reddit, Quora, and niche community forums are where people phrase their actual questions before Google has indexed them into a tracked keyword. Search your topic on Reddit and note the exact phrasing people use. These often surface variants with 200-800 monthly searches and KD scores under 20 that no keyword tool would surface through standard seed-based research.

This method is time-intensive but produces keywords with strong conversion potential because you're matching the exact language your audience uses when they have a specific problem.

### Method 6: Topic cluster long-tail expansion

If you have existing pillar content or are planning a cluster, map the pillar keyword's semantic variants explicitly. For a pillar targeting "keyword research," the supporting articles should each target a long-tail variant: "how to find keywords for a new website," "keyword research for small business," "free keyword research methods." These variants are almost always lower competition than the pillar because they're more specific and fewer authoritative sites have bothered to create dedicated pages for them.

This is the method that scales. Each cluster you build generates a list of low competition supporting article targets automatically.

## How to verify a keyword is actually low competition (4-step SERP audit)

Keyword difficulty scores are estimates, not verdicts. A KD score can be wrong by 15-25 points in either direction -- and tools frequently underestimate competition for keywords where high-DA sites rank from topical authority rather than backlinks.

Before committing writing resources to a low competition candidate, spend five minutes on this SERP audit.

**Step 1: Check page-one domain authority distribution**

Search the keyword in an incognito window and check the domain authority of the top 5-7 results using a tool like Moz Bar or Ahrefs Toolbar. If the top results are all DA 70+, the keyword is harder than the KD score suggests -- those sites rank from topical authority, not backlinks. If there's a mix including DA 20-40 sites, the keyword is genuinely accessible.

**Step 2: Count referring domains to the ranking pages**

Look at the number of referring domains pointing to each page-one result (not domain authority -- page-level backlink count). If the top 3 results each have 200+ referring domains, you'll need comparable links to compete. If they have 5-20 referring domains, strong content quality can rank without aggressive link-building.

**Step 3: Assess content quality and freshness**

Click through the top 3 results and assess honestly: are these well-written, comprehensive pages published in the last two years? If the current page-one results are thin, outdated (pre-2023), or clearly AI-generated without original analysis, strong original content has a structural advantage regardless of the domain authority behind it.

**Step 4: Identify topical authority traps**

Some keywords appear low competition in tools because the pages ranking there have few backlinks -- but they're owned by DA 80+ domains that rank via topical authority. A NY Times article on "expense tracking for freelancers" may have 3 referring domains but be nearly impossible to displace because Google trusts the domain broadly. If all top results are from major publications, manually check whether they have thin content you can definitively outperform before committing.

If a keyword passes all four steps, it's a genuine low competition opportunity. If it fails step 1 or step 4, move it to a "revisit at DA 40+" list.

## How to build a low competition keyword strategy

Finding individual low competition keywords is the first step. The compounding effect comes from building them into a deliberate cluster strategy.

**Step 1: Set your DA-adjusted KD ceiling**

Based on your current domain authority, define the maximum KD you'll target for new content. DA 0-20: KD ceiling 25. DA 21-40: KD ceiling 40. DA 41-60: KD ceiling 55. Review this ceiling every quarter as your authority grows.

**Step 2: Build candidate lists by cluster, not by individual keyword**

Group every candidate keyword by topic before evaluating individual targets. "Low competition keywords about expense tracking for small businesses" is a cluster. "Expense tracking for construction companies," "expense tracking for freelancers," "expense tracking for nonprofits" are three supporting articles in that cluster. Targeting them together builds topical authority faster than targeting them independently months apart.

**Step 3: Prioritize by intent stage**

Within a cluster, publish awareness and informational content (high volume, low KD) first to build domain relevance, then publish consideration and commercial-intent content (lower volume, slightly higher KD but conversion-valuable). The early informational pages build topical authority that helps the commercial pages rank.

**Step 4: Write in publishing sprints, not drips**

Publish 3-5 articles in a cluster within 2-4 weeks rather than spreading them out over months. Google registers topical authority faster when related content appears in clusters. Publishing the entire Cluster 3 keyword research articles simultaneously creates stronger relevance signals than publishing them six weeks apart.

**Step 5: Add a targeted link to each new article from an existing page**

Low competition doesn't mean zero links needed -- it means less. When you publish a new low competition article, add one internal link to it from an existing high-traffic page on the same topic. This passes page authority to the new article and accelerates indexation.

## FAQ

**How many monthly searches should a low competition keyword have?**

There is no universal minimum. A keyword with 150 monthly searches that converts at 5% is more valuable than a keyword with 1,500 searches that converts at 0.3%. For content-first agencies and SaaS companies, a minimum of 100-200 monthly searches is a practical floor -- below that, the cumulative cluster traffic is too thin to justify the writing investment. For local businesses, even 50-search terms can be worth targeting if the intent is commercial.

**Can I find low competition keywords without paid tools?**

Yes, with reduced efficiency. Google Search Console provides your existing rankings data (free). Google autocomplete and People Also Ask (free) surface real query phrasing. Ubersuggest and Keyword Surfer offer limited free KD estimates. The limitation of free tools is incomplete data -- you'll miss opportunities and misjudge some competitive levels. For sites serious about SEO, a $99/mo Ahrefs or Semrush subscription typically pays for itself with a single additional ranked keyword.

**How long does it take to rank for a low competition keyword?**

For keywords with KD under 20, a new site publishing quality content can realistically expect page-one rankings in 60-90 days based on Ahrefs benchmark data. For KD 20-35, expect 3-6 months. These timelines assume the page passes the 4-step SERP audit and the content genuinely outperforms current page-one results in depth, recency, and user experience.

**What's the difference between low competition and low volume?**

Low competition describes the ranking difficulty, not the traffic size. A keyword can have high competition and low volume (a niche technical term that major publications have already targeted) or low competition and meaningful volume (a specific question nobody has written a dedicated page for). The sweet spot is low competition AND meaningful volume -- which is what the 6 methods above are designed to find.

**Should I stop targeting low competition keywords once my domain authority grows?**

No. Domain authority growth means your KD ceiling rises -- you can win KD 50 keywords that were previously inaccessible. But the strategy of targeting systematically undervalued opportunities doesn't change. The most efficient content programs at any domain authority level are built around keywords where the current SERP is beatable. Professional keyword research is not about targeting hard keywords; it's about finding accessible opportunities others have missed.

## Conclusion

Low competition keywords are not a beginner workaround. They're the deliberate targeting of search terms where your content can realistically rank, generate traffic, and build the domain authority that eventually opens higher-competition opportunities.

The process is systematic: set your DA-adjusted KD ceiling, find candidates using the 6 methods, verify competition with the 4-step SERP audit, and build your targets into clusters rather than isolated articles. A portfolio of 15 pages ranking position 3-7 for KD 10-25 keywords compounds into thousands of monthly visitors faster -- and with more predictability -- than chasing high-competition terms and ranking nowhere.
