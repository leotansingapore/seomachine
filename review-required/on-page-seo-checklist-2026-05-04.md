# On-page SEO checklist: 43 checks to run before you hit publish

On-page SEO is the process of optimizing individual web pages so they rank higher and earn more organic traffic from search engines. The factors you control include title tags, heading structure, content quality, keyword placement, internal links, image optimization, and page speed -- applied consistently across every page you publish.

Most teams don't have a system. They write an article, optimize the title, maybe add a meta description, and call it done. That's why 96.55% of published pages get zero organic traffic, according to Ahrefs. The pages that rank have 30-40 on-page elements working together. Miss half of them, and you're leaving positions -- and revenue -- on the table.

This checklist covers every on-page SEO factor that moves the needle, organized into six categories and prioritized by impact. Run it before every publish. It takes 15 minutes once you've done it a few times.

> **Key takeaways**
> - Title tags are the single highest-impact on-page element: keyword in the first 60 characters, unique on every page, benefit-focused.
> - Meta descriptions don't affect rankings directly -- they affect CTR, which does. Write them like ads, not summaries.
> - Heading hierarchy (H1 > H2 > H3, no skipped levels) helps both crawlers and readers understand page structure.
> - Internal links distribute PageRank across your site; 3-5 contextual internal links per article is the target range.
> - Core Web Vitals (LCP, CLS, INP) are confirmed Google ranking factors. Pages in the bottom quartile lose ground to technically cleaner competitors.

## Category 1: Title tag and meta elements

These are the first thing Google reads. Get them wrong and nothing else matters.

### Title tag (6 checks)

**1. Primary keyword near the front of the title.**
Google front-loads title tags in its weighting. "On-Page SEO Checklist: 43 Checks for 2026" outperforms "The Complete Guide to On-Page SEO for 2026" for the keyword `on-page SEO checklist` -- the keyword appears in position one, not position eight.

**2. Title is 50-60 characters.**
Google truncates titles over 60 characters in SERPs. Your title may display in full on desktop but cut off on mobile, removing the context a searcher needs to click. Aim for 55-58 characters as your target zone.

**3. Title is unique across your entire site.**
Duplicate titles confuse crawlers and split ranking signals. Every page needs a title that couldn't belong to any other page on your domain.

**4. Title matches the page's actual content.**
Clickbait titles that don't deliver on their promise increase bounce rate and pogo-sticking -- both signals Google uses to assess page quality. If your title says "43 checks," the article needs 43 checks.

**5. Title includes a year for time-sensitive topics.**
"On-Page SEO Checklist 2026" outperforms "On-Page SEO Checklist" for any query that includes a year modifier. Searchers click year-dated results at significantly higher rates for how-to and checklist content.

**6. Title is compelling, not just accurate.**
"On-Page SEO Checklist" is accurate. "On-Page SEO Checklist: 43 Checks (Prioritized by Impact)" is accurate and specific. Specificity -- number of items, year, qualifier -- increases CTR by giving searchers more signal before they click.

### Meta description (4 checks)

**7. Meta description is 150-160 characters.**
Under 150, you're leaving SERP real estate empty. Over 160, Google truncates mid-sentence and your CTA disappears.

**8. Primary keyword is in the meta description.**
Google bolds keywords in meta descriptions that match the search query. A description that includes your keyword gets more visual weight in the SERP -- higher CTR without any ranking movement.

**9. Meta description directly answers the target query.**
For AI search optimization, your meta description needs to function as a direct answer, not a teaser. If someone asks "what is an on-page SEO checklist," the meta description should answer that question in 150 characters.

**10. Meta description includes a clear CTA.**
"Learn, Discover, Get, Find out" -- use an action verb. Passive descriptions ("This guide covers...") convert at lower CTR than active ones ("Learn the 43 on-page SEO checks that drive rankings").

### URL slug (3 checks)

**11. URL includes the primary keyword.**
`/blog/on-page-seo-checklist` outperforms `/blog/a-complete-guide-to-optimizing-your-pages` for every ranking factor. Keyword in URL is a confirmed (minor) ranking signal and a major clarity signal for users previewing a link.

**12. URL is short and readable.**
3-5 words. Remove stop words (the, a, and, of) unless they're necessary for readability. `/blog/on-page-seo-checklist` not `/blog/the-complete-guide-to-on-page-seo-optimization-for-beginners`.

**13. URL uses hyphens, not underscores.**
Google treats hyphens as word separators. Underscores are treated as connectors. `on-page-seo` is three words to Google; `on_page_seo` reads as one.

## Category 2: Heading structure

A well-structured article is easier for Google to parse and easier for humans to skim. Both outcomes improve rankings.

**14. One H1, and it contains the primary keyword.**
One H1 per page, full stop. Multiple H1s send mixed signals about what the page is about. The H1 should include your primary keyword -- ideally near the beginning.

**15. H2s cover the major subtopics of the page.**
Think of H2s as the chapter titles. A reader should be able to skim your H2s and understand the full scope of the article. 4-7 H2s is the target range for a 2,500-3,000 word article.

**16. At least 2-3 H2s include keyword variations.**
If your primary keyword is `on-page SEO checklist`, relevant H2 variations include "On-page SEO factors ranked by impact" or "How to run an on-page SEO audit on your pages." These variations capture semantic search intent without keyword stuffing.

**17. No skipped heading levels.**
H1 > H2 > H3 -- never H1 > H3 or H2 > H4. Skipped levels break the page outline for screen readers and may confuse Googlebot's understanding of content hierarchy.

**18. H3s break complex H2 sections into components.**
When an H2 section covers multiple related points, H3s provide structure. This is the pattern that earns featured snippet positions for list-based queries.

## Category 3: Content quality and keyword optimization

This is where most on-page SEO checklists go wrong -- they treat content as a keyword placement exercise. Google's systems are more sophisticated. Keyword placement matters, but topical coverage and content depth matter more.

### Keyword placement (5 checks)

**19. Primary keyword in the first 100 words.**
Google gives more weight to content near the top of the page. Your keyword should appear naturally within the first paragraph -- not forced, not repeated, just present.

**20. Primary keyword density is 1-2%.**
In a 2,500-word article, that's 25-50 uses. Not 200. Keyword stuffing triggers a spam classifier; too-low density misses semantic signals. 1-2% is the target range, with natural variation in phrasing (on-page SEO, on-page optimization, on-page factors).

**21. Secondary keywords are distributed throughout.**
Secondary keywords should appear in H2s, body paragraphs, and the conclusion. They don't need the density of your primary keyword -- one or two natural mentions each is sufficient.

**22. Keyword appears in the conclusion.**
The last paragraph of your article is a high-weight zone -- Google uses it as a signal of topical completion. Include the primary keyword naturally in your conclusion or final H2.

**23. No keyword stuffing.**
If a sentence reads unnaturally because a keyword was forced into it, rewrite the sentence. Keyword stuffing is a ranking penalty, not a ranking signal.

### Content depth (5 checks)

**24. Article covers the topic comprehensively.**
Pull the top 10 SERP results for your target keyword. List the H2s they use. Your article should cover everything the top-ranking articles cover, plus something they miss. Thin coverage is the most common on-page failure.

**25. Article matches search intent.**
If the SERP for your keyword shows listicles and checklists, write a listicle or checklist. If it shows long-form guides, write a guide. Google's ranking algorithm has already solved for the format searchers want -- don't fight it.

**26. Article includes a direct answer in the first 1-2 sentences.**
AI search engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews) pull from the top of the page. Burying your main answer 500 words in means you miss every AI citation opportunity. State your direct answer first, then develop it.

**27. Article includes a Key Takeaways block.**
A TL;DR block immediately after the introduction helps AI scrapers summarize your content accurately. It also reduces bounce rate -- readers who see the takeaways and stay are higher-quality engagement signals.

**28. Article word count is competitive with SERP leaders.**
For most competitive keywords, you need to be within 20% of the word count of the top three ranking articles. Use our [SEO content strategy](https://leotanagency.com/blog/seo-content-strategy) framework to benchmark before you write, not after.

### E-E-A-T signals (4 checks)

**29. Article has a named author.**
Google's E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) framework weights authorship. Anonymous content scores zero on trustworthiness. Named authors with credentials signal that a human expert stands behind the content.

**30. Author bio includes relevant credentials.**
The author bio should establish why this person is qualified to write on this topic. Two sentences with specific credentials ("Leo Tan has managed SEO for 40+ companies across SaaS, e-commerce, and B2B") signals expertise to both Google and the reader.

**31. Statistics and claims are cited with sources.**
Every statistic should link to its original source. "96.55% of pages get zero traffic" without a link to Ahrefs is an unverifiable claim. Cited statistics build trustworthiness and add external link context.

**32. Content includes a "last updated" date.**
For evergreen content, Google tracks freshness. An article updated in 2026 ranks better for time-sensitive queries than an identical article last touched in 2023. Add the "last updated" date visibly on the page -- not just in metadata.

## Category 4: Internal and external linking

### Internal links (4 checks)

**33. Article includes 3-5 internal links.**
Internal links pass PageRank from high-authority pages to newer or lower-authority pages. They also help Google understand the topical relationship between your pages -- a signal that builds cluster authority over time.

**34. Anchor text is descriptive and keyword-rich.**
"Click here" is dead. Anchor text is a relevance signal. "Our guide to running a [technical SEO audit](https://leotanagency.com/blog/technical-seo-audit)" tells Google that the linked page is about technical SEO audits. Generic anchor text wastes the signal.

**35. Internal links are contextually placed.**
A link dropped into a sentence because it fits is more valuable than a link dropped into a sidebar. Body paragraph links in context pass more authority than navigation links.

**36. Article links to its pillar page.**
In a topic cluster model, every cluster article links back to the pillar. This is how topical authority compounds: cluster articles pull in long-tail traffic and funnel PageRank up to the pillar, which then ranks for the high-volume head term.

### External links (3 checks)

**37. Article includes 2-3 external links to authoritative sources.**
External links to credible sources (Google's documentation, Ahrefs, Moz, government data) are a trustworthiness signal. They also protect you against citing unverifiable claims.

**38. External links open in a new tab.**
Readers who click an external link and leave your site don't come back. Use `target="_blank"` on external links to keep your session active.

**39. No broken external links.**
A broken external link signals neglect to both users and crawlers. Run a link check before every publish and schedule quarterly checks on published content.

## Category 5: Image optimization

Images are often the biggest missed on-page opportunity. Five minutes of image optimization can recover significant page speed, which directly affects rankings.

**40. Every image has descriptive alt text.**
Alt text serves two functions: accessibility (screen readers) and SEO (Google can't "see" images, it reads alt text). Describe what the image shows in 125 characters or less, including your keyword where it's genuinely relevant.

**41. Image file names are descriptive.**
`on-page-seo-checklist-title-tag-example.jpg` is better than `IMG_4531.jpg`. File names are a minor on-page signal and a major readability improvement for the URL structure of your media library.

**42. Images are compressed for web.**
Unoptimized images are the most common cause of failing Core Web Vitals scores. Target JPEG files under 150KB, PNG under 200KB. Use WebP where your CMS supports it -- typically 30-40% smaller than JPEG at equivalent quality.

**43. Images have appropriate dimensions.**
An image displayed at 600px wide shouldn't be uploaded at 2,400px. The browser downloads the full file and scales it down, wasting bandwidth and hurting page speed for zero visual benefit.

## Category 6: Core Web Vitals and technical on-page factors

On-page SEO doesn't stop at content. Technical performance is confirmed ranking signal territory. Run a [technical SEO audit](https://leotanagency.com/blog/technical-seo-audit) alongside your on-page checklist -- the two work together, not in isolation.

According to [Google's Core Web Vitals guidance](https://web.dev/articles/lcp), pages in the "Good" CWV range (LCP under 2.5s, CLS under 0.1, INP under 200ms) rank measurably better than pages in the "Needs Improvement" or "Poor" ranges, particularly for competitive queries.

**LCP (Largest Contentful Paint):**
- Target: Under 2.5 seconds
- Most common cause of LCP failure: unoptimized hero images or render-blocking JavaScript
- Fix: Preload your largest above-the-fold image, defer non-critical JS

**CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift):**
- Target: Under 0.1
- Most common cause: Images without defined width/height attributes, ads that load after the page
- Fix: Always define image dimensions in HTML; reserve space for ad slots

**INP (Interaction to Next Paint):**
- Target: Under 200ms
- Most common cause: Heavy JavaScript execution blocking the main thread
- Fix: Audit and defer third-party scripts (chat widgets, analytics, retargeting pixels)

## How to run this checklist efficiently

Don't run this checklist manually for every page. Build it into your publishing workflow:

1. **Pre-write**: Confirm keyword, intent match, and target word count before drafting.
2. **Post-draft**: Check categories 1-3 (meta elements, headings, content/keywords).
3. **Pre-publish**: Check categories 4-6 (links, images, CWV).
4. **Post-publish**: Verify meta rendering in Google Search Console after indexation.

That's four checkpoints, not 43 simultaneous tasks. Splitting the checklist by workflow stage reduces cognitive load and catches errors at the right moment.

### Mini-story: the optimization that moved a page from 14 to 4

When we ran the full on-page audit, we found three issues: the title was 74 characters (truncated on mobile), there were zero internal links from or to the page (it was an orphan), and the hero image was 1.8MB -- causing the page to fail LCP at 6.2 seconds.

Fixes took 45 minutes:

- Three contextual internal links added (one from a related post, two from this article to cluster content)
- Image compressed to 140KB, LCP dropped to 1.9 seconds

Result: position 14 to position 4 in 38 days. No new content written. No link building. On-page fixes only.

This is what the checklist is actually for: finding the fixable things you can't see until you look systematically.

## FAQ: on-page SEO checklist

**What is on-page SEO?**
On-page SEO is the practice of optimizing elements within a web page -- including title tags, headings, content, images, and internal links -- to improve its ranking in search engine results. Unlike off-page SEO (backlinks, brand mentions), on-page SEO is entirely within your control.

**How long does on-page SEO take to show results?**
On-page changes typically produce measurable ranking movement within two to eight weeks, depending on how frequently Google crawls your site. Pages with existing impressions (tracked in Google Search Console) respond faster than pages with no ranking history.

**Should I do on-page SEO before or after publishing?**
Both. Pre-publish: run categories 1-4 of this checklist before hitting publish. Post-publish: monitor CTR and ranking data in Google Search Console, then revisit any page ranking in positions 11-20 -- those are your highest-ROI optimization targets.

**How many keywords should I target per page?**
One primary keyword and three to five secondary keywords per page. Targeting multiple high-competition primary keywords on one page fragments your ranking signals and typically results in ranking for none of them. One page, one primary intent.

**Does meta description affect rankings?**
Meta descriptions are not a direct ranking factor. They affect CTR, which is a ranking factor. A compelling meta description that earns more clicks on the same SERP position sends a positive signal to Google. Write meta descriptions as ads, not summaries.
