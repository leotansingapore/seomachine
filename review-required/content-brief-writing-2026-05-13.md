# How to Write a Content Brief That Writers Actually Use

A content brief is a document that tells a writer everything they need to produce a piece of content without back-and-forth: the target keyword, the intended audience, the required sections, the tone, the internal links to include, and the goal of the piece.

A well-written brief produces a first draft that's 80% of the way there. A bad brief -- or no brief -- produces a draft that requires 2-3 rounds of revision, misses keyword targets, and still doesn't answer the actual search intent.

Most teams know they need briefs. Where they fail is in how they write them.

> **Key Takeaways**
> - A content brief is the single highest-leverage document in a content operation -- 45 minutes on the brief saves 2-3 hours of revision per article
> - Most briefs fail because they're written as data dumps: technically complete but formatted in ways writers ignore
> - A complete SEO content brief has 7 components; most briefs include 3 or 4 and leave the rest implicit
> - The brief should explain the "why" behind each instruction, not just list requirements
> - Brief quality is the primary driver of first-draft quality -- not writer skill

---

## What is a content brief?

A content brief is a structured document that defines the requirements for a piece of content before the writer starts. It sits between the content strategy (what topics to cover, why) and the content itself (the article, landing page, or email).

The brief serves two purposes:

1. **Alignment**: ensures the writer produces content that matches the strategic goal, not just content that's grammatically correct and on-topic
2. **Efficiency**: moves decisions upstream so writers can draft without stopping to ask questions, and editors can approve without explaining why the draft missed the target

A content brief is not an outline. An outline shows structure. A brief shows requirements. The writer creates the outline using the brief as input.

Brief scope varies by content type. An SEO article brief is the most detailed: it includes keyword data, SERP context, competitor gaps, and technical SEO requirements. A product page brief is shorter but adds conversion requirements. A thought leadership brief prioritizes POV and distribution channel.

This guide focuses on SEO article briefs -- the most common type in a content operations system.

## Why most content briefs get ignored

The failure mode isn't writers forgetting to read the brief. It's briefs that are technically complete but written in ways that make ignoring them rational.

A brief that arrives as a spreadsheet with 40 rows of keyword data, a 600-word "context" section that reads like a strategy deck, and instructions like "ensure the article aligns with our brand voice" will be skimmed for the title and word count and then mostly disregarded.

Three patterns produce unusable briefs:

**1. The data dump.** Exporting keyword research directly into the brief without synthesis. The writer gets 15 keyword variants and no instruction on how to use them. The primary keyword gets buried. Semantic keywords get treated as a list to insert, not a signal of what questions to answer.

**2. Missing the "why."** Instructions without reasoning are ignored or misapplied. "Write at a 7th-grade reading level" without context produces oversimplified content. "The target audience is mid-market CTOs" with no description of what those CTOs actually need produces generic content that feels targeted.

**3. No clear success criteria.** Writers can't self-edit without knowing what "done" looks like. A brief that defines success as "a comprehensive article on X" will produce comprehensive articles that still miss the mark.

## What to include in an SEO content brief (7 components)

### 1. Keyword brief

The keyword brief tells the writer how to handle search intent. It's not a dump of everything from your [keyword research](/blog/keyword-research-for-seo) -- it's a synthesized instruction set.

**What to include**:
- Primary keyword (exact match) -- the one term the article is optimized to rank for
- Secondary keywords (3-5) -- semantic variants that indicate sub-topics to cover
- Search intent classification (informational, commercial, transactional) -- defines the goal of the searcher, which defines the goal of the article
- SERP format signal -- are the current top results long-form articles, listicles, comparison tables? Match the dominant format.

**What not to include**: Full keyword export, volume data, CPC data, keyword difficulty scores. Those belong in the strategy layer. The brief gets the instructions, not the research.

### 2. Audience definition

One paragraph, specific. Job title, company type, where they are in the buying or learning journey, what they already know that you can skip, and what they're actually trying to solve.

Bad: "Marketing managers at B2B companies."

Good: "Content managers at 50-200-person B2B SaaS companies who are publishing 2-4 articles per month and have a team of 1-3 writers. They know basic SEO but haven't systematized briefing. They're reading this because they're frustrated with inconsistent draft quality and revision cycles."

The second version tells the writer the knowledge baseline (skip the SEO 101 definitions), the specific pain point (revision cycles), and the emotional state (frustrated). Those three inputs change the tone and content of the article.

### 3. Article angle

The angle is the specific position the article takes that differentiates it from the 10 articles already ranking. Stating the angle in the brief forces the content strategist to identify differentiation before briefing -- which is the right order.

Format: "Our angle is [X] because [competitor articles] miss/ignore/misframe [Y]. We will argue/show/prove [Z]."

Example: "Our angle is that most content briefs fail because of format problems, not information gaps. Competitor articles (Ahrefs, HubSpot, Semrush) treat brief-writing as an information assembly problem. We argue it's a communication design problem -- the brief has to be readable by a writer under time pressure, not comprehensive for a strategist in a planning session."

### 4. Required sections / structure

Not a full outline -- the writer writes that. A required sections list ensures critical topics aren't skipped and signals the intended structure.

Format: a bulleted list of H2-level topics that must be covered, with 1-2 sentences on what each section should accomplish.

Example:
- **What is a content brief?** -- definition, distinguish from outline, scope by content type. Should be scannable in 60 seconds.
- **Why most content briefs get ignored** -- the failure mode before the solution. This section validates the reader's experience before giving them the fix.
- **7 components** -- the core deliverable. Format as sub-sections (H3 per component). Each component needs: what it is, what to include, and one example of good vs. bad.

### 5. Internal links

List the exact pages to link, with anchor text suggestions. The writer should not be researching internal links while drafting -- they'll either skip them or pick poor anchors.

Format: `[URL] -- [suggested anchor text] -- [where to place it naturally]`

Example:
- `/blog/seo-content-strategy` -- "SEO content strategy" -- when introducing the strategy-to-execution chain in the intro
- `/blog/content-gap-analysis` -- "content gap analysis" -- in the keyword brief section when referencing how keyword targets are identified

### 6. Differentiation notes

A bullet list of specific things the article must do or say that competitor articles don't. This is where [content gap analysis](/blog/content-gap-analysis) output goes -- translated from "what competitors miss" to "what our article must include."

3-5 bullets is the right length. More than 5 and the writer is managing a checklist instead of writing.

### 7. Success criteria

A 3-5 sentence description of what a complete, approved draft looks like. This is what the writer uses to self-edit before submitting, and what the editor uses to approve or return.

Example: "The draft passes when: (1) a content manager can open the 7 components section, copy the template, and start a brief in under 10 minutes; (2) the 'why most briefs get ignored' section matches the reader's experience without hedging; (3) every instruction in the article is followed by an example that makes the instruction concrete; (4) no section uses the phrase 'it's important to' or 'make sure to' without a reason."

## How to write a content brief step by step

This is the process that produces a usable brief in 30-45 minutes:

**Step 1: Pull the keyword brief from your SEO content strategy.**
The article topic and primary keyword should already be defined in your [SEO content strategy](/blog/seo-content-strategy) or content calendar. The brief pulls from the strategy -- it doesn't create it. If you're defining the keyword in the brief, you've skipped a step.

**Step 2: Run a 5-result SERP audit.**
Open the top 5 results for the primary keyword. Scan each for: format (article, listicle, guide, tool), length (rough word count), sub-topics covered, and what they don't cover or get wrong. 15 minutes. This produces the angle and differentiation notes.

**Step 3: Write the audience definition.**
Before touching the keyword data, write the one-paragraph audience definition. This is the lens through which every other decision gets made. If you can't write a specific audience definition, the article topic isn't specific enough.

**Step 4: Fill in the 7 components.**
Work through the components in order. The keyword brief first, then audience, then angle (which requires both). Sections and internal links are mechanical once the first three are clear.

**Step 5: Write the success criteria last.**
Success criteria can only be written once you know what the article has to do. Write it after the rest of the brief is complete. If you can't write specific success criteria, the brief is still too vague.

## Content brief template

Copy this structure for every SEO article brief:

```
---
PRIMARY KEYWORD:
SECONDARY KEYWORDS (3-5):
SEARCH INTENT:
SERP FORMAT:
URL SLUG:
TARGET WORD COUNT:
---

## Audience
[One paragraph -- job title, company type, knowledge level, specific pain point]

## Angle
[One sentence -- what we argue that competitors don't, and why]

## Required sections
- [H2 topic]: [What this section must accomplish]
- [H2 topic]: [What this section must accomplish]
- [continue for all required H2s]

## Internal links
- [URL] -- [anchor text] -- [placement note]
- [URL] -- [anchor text] -- [placement note]

## Differentiation notes
- [Specific thing our article must include that competitors miss]
- [continue for 3-5 bullets]

## Success criteria
[3-5 sentences defining what a complete, approved draft looks like]
```

## FAQ

**How long should a content brief be?**
For a standard 2,000-3,000 word SEO article, a brief should be 400-700 words. Long enough to be complete, short enough to be read. If your brief is over 1,000 words, you're writing the article instead of briefing it.

**Should you include an outline in the brief?**
No. The brief defines what the article must cover and why. The writer creates the outline from the brief. Providing an outline in the brief removes the writer's structural judgment -- which is part of what you're paying for -- and produces templated writing.

**Who should write the content brief?**
The content strategist or SEO lead, not the writer. The brief requires SERP research, keyword context, and strategic alignment that the writer typically doesn't have. Asking a writer to self-brief produces articles that are well-written but strategically misaligned.

**Can you use AI to write content briefs?**
AI can populate the template once you've defined the angle and audience -- it's fast at generating required sections from a keyword and producing internal link suggestions. But the angle and success criteria need human judgment. AI-generated angles produce generic differentiation claims because the model doesn't know what competitors actually got wrong.

**How often should you update a content brief template?**
Review and update your template every 6 months or after any significant change in how Google's ranking systems evaluate content. The core components are stable, but weighting shifts. Core Web Vitals, for example, became a required component after 2021 and wasn't in most templates before that.
